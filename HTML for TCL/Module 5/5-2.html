Provider-Specific Tips

Every test provider has quirks. Knowing them in advance turns surprises into advantages — from SHL's layout to Arctic Shores' gamified traps.

1

Why Provider Knowledge Matters

Knowing the content is only half the battle. Each provider's test has a different interface, timing structure, and question style. Candidates who've practised on the right platform perform 15–20% better than those using generic prep materials.

This lesson covers the five major providers used by UK finance employers. Find your target employer in Lesson 1.2's lookup table, then focus on the relevant provider section below.

SHL (CEB/Gartner)
Used by: Barclays, Deloitte, EY, Goldman Sachs, HSBC, JP Morgan, KPMG, PwC, Rothschild

Numerical: 18 questions, 25 minutes. Data presented as tables, charts, or combined. Questions appear one at a time. You CAN go back to previous questions — use this for the 3-pass method.

Verbal: 30 questions, 19 minutes. Passage + statement format. You cannot go back. Commit to your answer and move on. The statement-first technique from Lesson 4.4 is essential.

Interface tip: SHL's on-screen calculator is basic. It doesn't support brackets or memory. For multi-step calculations, write intermediate results on scratch paper rather than trying to chain them in the calculator.

Key quirk: SHL tests use a norm comparison — your raw score is compared to a norm group (e.g., "graduate professionals"). Scoring 70% correct might put you at the 85th percentile or the 50th, depending on the norm group. Focus on accuracy, not speed.

Aon (formerly cut-e)
Used by: UBS, BNP Paribas, Credit Suisse (legacy), Standard Chartered, Aviva

Format: Adaptive — questions get harder when you answer correctly, easier when you answer wrong. Your score reflects the difficulty level you sustain, not just the number correct.

Key difference: You cannot skip or go back. Each question must be answered before the next appears. This makes the 3-pass method impossible — you need a different strategy.

Strategy: Don't spend more than 60 seconds per question. If you're stuck, make an educated guess and preserve your momentum. Getting stuck on a hard question and running out of time is worse than guessing one question wrong.

Interface tip: The Aon interface is minimalist. The timer is small and in the corner — glance at it every 3–4 questions to stay on pace.

Korn Ferry (formerly Hay Group / TalentQ)
Used by: Citi, Lloyds, Schroders, Zurich, AXA

Numerical: 24 questions, 25 minutes. Similar to SHL but with slightly more questions in the same time — faster pace required.

Verbal: Uses "select all that apply" format rather than T/F/CS. You may need to select 2–3 correct statements from a list of 5–6. Partial credit may apply.

Key quirk: Korn Ferry often uses "drag and drop" answers in their newer formats. Practice clicking/dragging on their interface before the real test — technical familiarity reduces anxiety.

Strategy: Their practice test portal is free. Use it. The interface is different enough from SHL that you'll lose 10–15 seconds per question on unfamiliar navigation if you haven't practised.

Cappfinity (Strengths-Based)
Used by: HSBC, Barclays, Unilever, Civil Service

Format: Primarily strengths-based and SJT assessments rather than pure aptitude. Questions ask about your natural reactions, preferences, and behaviours.

Key difference: There are no "right" answers in the traditional sense. The test measures whether your natural strengths align with the role's requirements.

Strategy: Answer quickly and honestly. Cappfinity measures response time — hesitation suggests you're calculating rather than responding naturally. First-instinct answers score best.

Warning: Do NOT try to game strengths-based assessments. They have consistency checks built in — answering contradictory questions differently raises a red flag.

Arctic Shores (Gamified)
Used by: Citi, Siemens, Unilever, JP Morgan (some divisions)

Format: Gamified tasks instead of traditional MCQs. Tasks measure cognitive traits (e.g., risk tolerance, processing speed, working memory) through game-like scenarios.

Key insight: There's no way to "prepare" for the specific games — they change regularly. What you CAN prepare is your mindset: be decisive, avoid excessive caution, and don't try to reverse-engineer what the game measures.

Speed matters: Unlike traditional tests, Arctic Shores measures HOW you make decisions, not just WHAT you decide. Fast, consistent decision-making scores better than slow, agonised choices.

Strategy: Treat it like a real-time simulation. Engage naturally. The games are designed to be impossible to fake.

Key Takeaways

  • SHL: You can navigate back — use the 3-pass method. Calculator is basic; use scratch paper for multi-step work.
  • Aon: Adaptive and no going back. Don't stall — guess and preserve momentum over agonising on one question.
  • Korn Ferry: Faster pace and "select all" verbal format. Practise on their free portal for interface familiarity.
  • Cappfinity: Strengths-based — answer quickly and honestly. Don't try to game it.
  • Arctic Shores: Gamified — decisiveness and consistency matter more than "correctness." Be natural.
