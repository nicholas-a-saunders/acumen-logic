Situational Judgement Tests

SJTs measure how you'd behave at work — not what you know. Learn the formats, the finance-sector values that guide "best" answers, and the elimination method that works every time.

1

What SJTs Actually Measure

Situational Judgement Tests present realistic workplace scenarios and ask you to choose the best (or worst) response from several options. Unlike aptitude tests, there's no calculation — just judgement.

SJTs measure competencies: teamwork, communication, integrity, client focus, problem-solving, and leadership. Finance employers weight these differently, but integrity and client focus always rank highest.

2

The Three SJT Formats

Format 1 — "Most Effective / Least Effective": You're given 4 response options and must identify the most effective AND the least effective. Used by SHL and Korn Ferry.

Format 2 — "Rank Order": Rank all 4 options from most to least appropriate. Partial credit for partially correct rankings. Used by Cappfinity and Civil Service.

Format 3 — "Rate Each": Rate each option independently (e.g., "Very Effective / Effective / Slightly Effective / Ineffective"). Used by some bespoke assessments.

Critical Insight

In Format 1, the "least effective" is often more discriminating than the "most effective." Most candidates identify the best answer correctly — the least effective choice is where marks are won or lost.

3

Finance-Sector Values Hierarchy

SJT answers in finance roles are graded against a specific values framework. Understanding this hierarchy lets you predict the "correct" answer even in unfamiliar scenarios.

PriorityValueWhat It Means in Practice
1Regulatory compliance & integrityAlways follow rules, escalate concerns, never cut corners on compliance
2Client interestClient outcomes take priority over internal convenience
3Risk managementIdentify, escalate, and mitigate risks proactively
4Team collaborationSupport colleagues, share information, seek help when needed
5Professional developmentLearn from mistakes, seek feedback, develop skills
6EfficiencyImportant, but never at the expense of values 1–5
The #1 SJT Mistake

Choosing the "efficient" answer over the "compliant" one. In finance SJTs, the answer that saves time but skips a compliance step is always wrong. Even if the scenario feels low-risk.

4

The Elimination Method

4-Step SJT Method
Read scenario → Identify the competency → Eliminate extremes → Apply the values hierarchy

Step 1: Read the scenario carefully. Identify: What's the problem? Who's involved? What's the context?

Step 2: Identify which competency is being tested. Is it about integrity? Teamwork? Client focus? This tells you what the "right" answer will prioritise.

Step 3: Eliminate the two extreme options. One will be obviously passive (doing nothing, ignoring the problem). One will be obviously aggressive (confrontation, going over heads without due process). These are almost always wrong.

Step 4: Between the remaining two, apply the values hierarchy. The answer that addresses the core issue while respecting compliance and relationships is the best.

Worked Example — SJT Analysis
You notice a colleague entering a client trade that appears to breach the firm's risk limits. The colleague is senior to you and appears to be in a rush. What do you do?

A. Say nothing — the colleague is senior and probably knows what they're doing.

B. Immediately report the colleague to the compliance department without speaking to them first.

C. Politely ask the colleague if they've checked the trade against risk limits, and if the issue persists, escalate to your manager.

D. Send an email to the entire team warning about risk limit breaches.

Analysis:

Eliminate A (passive — ignores a potential compliance breach). Eliminate D (aggressive — public shaming without addressing the individual). Between B and C: C addresses the issue directly with the individual first (respectful), then escalates if needed (compliant). B skips the direct conversation entirely.

Best: C. Least effective: A. Doing nothing about a potential compliance breach is always the worst option in finance.

5

Common SJT Traps

Trap: "Handle It Yourself"

Options that say "resolve the issue independently without involving anyone else" sound proactive but ignore the teamwork and escalation values. In finance, unilateral action is risky.

Better: Involve the Right People

Options that involve consulting a colleague, speaking to a manager, or seeking guidance. These demonstrate collaboration and appropriate escalation — key finance competencies.

Trap: "Wait and See"

Options like "monitor the situation and act if it gets worse" sound measured but are passive. In scenarios involving risk or compliance, waiting is almost always wrong.

Better: Act Promptly

Options that take immediate, proportionate action. Raise concerns early, seek clarification, address the issue now rather than waiting for escalation.

Key Takeaways

  • SJTs measure behaviour, not knowledge. There's no "studying" for them — but there IS a method for approaching them systematically.
  • In finance, compliance and integrity always win over efficiency, speed, or even client wishes.
  • Use the elimination method: remove the passive and aggressive extremes, then apply the values hierarchy to the remaining options.
  • The "least effective" choice is usually passive (doing nothing) or bypasses proper process. Look for it — it's worth as many marks as the best answer.
  • Don't overthink. SJTs have a time limit. Your first instinct after applying the method is usually correct.
