Bar Charts, Line Graphs & Pie Charts

SHL and Aon tests present data visually. You need to read values accurately, compare data points, and calculate from charts — all under time pressure.

1

Chart Types in Aptitude Tests

Four chart types dominate UK finance aptitude tests: grouped bar charts (comparing values across categories), stacked bar charts (showing composition), line graphs (tracking trends over time), and pie charts (showing proportions). Each has its own reading technique and its own specific trap.

The calculations are things you already know from Module 2. The new skill is extracting the right numbers from a visual before you calculate.

2

Grouped Bar Charts

Grouped bar charts place bars for multiple categories side by side. The key technique: use a straight edge (finger or pen) to trace horizontally from the top of a bar to the y-axis. Never estimate by eye alone — bars that look similar can differ by 10–15%, enough to change your answer.

Worked Example 1 — Direct Read "What was Bridgewell's revenue in 2024?"
1Locate the 2024 group of bars
2Identify Bridgewell (gold bar) within the group
3Trace horizontally to y-axis → the bar sits between 350 and 400, closer to 400
4Read value: £390k = £390,000

In a real test the value labels won't be shown. You'd estimate proportionally between gridlines: the bar is ~80% of the way from 350 to 400, so approximately 350 + (0.8 × 50) = 390.

Worked Example 2 — Comparison "By how much did Apex Ltd's revenue exceed Castleton's in 2023?"
1Apex 2023: £380k
2Castleton 2023: £260k
3Difference: £380k − £260k = £120,000
Worked Example 3 — Percentage Change from Chart "What was the percentage change in Bridgewell's revenue from 2022 to 2024?"
1Bridgewell 2022 (old): £280k
2Bridgewell 2024 (new): £390k
3Change: (390 − 280) ÷ 280 × 100 = 110 ÷ 280 = 39.3%

Divide by the OLD value (2022), not the new. Module 2 percentage change formula applied to chart data.

Reading Technique

On screen tests, you can't use a physical ruler. Instead: note the gridline values above and below the bar, then estimate proportionally. If gridlines are at 350 and 400, and the bar top is roughly four-fifths of the way up, the value is ~390.

Stacked Bar Charts — The Segment Trap

Stacked bar charts show composition — how a total breaks down into parts. They contain the single most common chart-reading error in aptitude tests: reading a middle segment's value as where its top sits on the y-axis, instead of subtracting the bottom from the top.

The Stacked Bar Rule
Segment Value = Top of Segment − Bottom of Segment
NEVER read the y-axis at the top of a middle segment. That includes everything below it.
Worked Example 4 — The Segment Trap "What were the Marketing costs in Q2?"

In the Q2 stacked bar, segments stack bottom-to-top: Salaries (0→185), Rent (185→280), Marketing (280→390), Other (390→455).

✗Wrong: Reading the top of Marketing on the y-axis → 390. This is Salaries + Rent + Marketing combined.
✓Correct: Top (390) − Bottom (280) = £110k
Common Mistake

"Marketing Q2 = £390k"

£390,000
Reading where the top of Marketing meets the y-axis. This is actually Salaries (£185k) + Rent (£95k) + Marketing (£110k) combined.
Correct Method

"Top of Marketing minus bottom"

£110,000
£390k (top) − £280k (bottom) = £110k. Always subtract for any segment that isn't the bottom one.
Quick Check

After calculating a segment, ask: does the value look proportional to its visual height? If Marketing visually occupies about a quarter of the bar but your answer is 80% of the total, you've read the cumulative y-axis position.

Line Graphs — Trends & Comparisons

Line graphs show change over time. The two most common question types: "In which period was the gap between X and Y greatest?" (comparison) and "What was the average value of X?" (aggregation). For both, read each data point carefully — don't just eyeball the trend.

Worked Example 5 — Largest Gap "In which month was the difference between UK and Europe traffic greatest?"

Calculate UK − Europe for each month:

1Jan: 45 − 30 = 15k  |  Feb: 52 − 35 = 17k  |  Mar: 48 − 38 = 10k
2Apr: 58 − 42 = 16k  |  May: 65 − 40 = 25k  |  Jun: 72 − 44 = 28k → June

Trap: May looks dramatic because Europe dips while UK surges. But June's gap (28k) exceeds May's (25k). Always calculate — don't just eyeball.

Worked Example 6 — Average Calculation "What was Asia's average monthly traffic across all six months?"
1Sum: 18 + 22 + 28 + 35 + 42 + 50 = 195k
2Average: 195 ÷ 6 = 32.5k = 32,500 visitors
Trend vs Value
Slope = Speed of Change  |  Position = Current Value
Asia's line is steepest (fastest growth) even though UK has the highest absolute traffic throughout.
5

Pie Charts — Proportions & Calculations

Pie charts show parts of a whole. Common tasks: (a) calculate a segment's value when given the total, (b) add segments for a merger scenario, or (c) work backwards from £ values to percentages. Key trap: if labels show £ values not %, you must calculate percentages yourself.

Chart 4 — UK Digital Payments Market Share
28% AlphaFin
22% BetaCorp
18% GammaGroup
15% DeltaLtd
17% Others

Total market value: £1.2 billion

Worked Example 7 — Value from Percentage "What is DeltaLtd's market value?"
1DeltaLtd share: 15%. Total market: £1.2bn = £1,200m
20.15 × £1,200m = £180 million
Worked Example 8 — Merger Combination "If AlphaFin and BetaCorp merge, what % of the market would the combined entity hold?"
128% + 22% = 50% (= £600m of £1.2bn)
The Reverse Pie Chart Trap

Some tests show pie labels as absolute values (£m), not percentages. If the chart shows "AlphaFin: £336m" you must first calculate the total, then derive the percentage: £336m ÷ £1,200m × 100 = 28%. Never assume pie labels are percentages — check whether they show £, units, or %.

Key Takeaways

  • Grouped bar charts: trace from bar top horizontally to the y-axis. Estimate between gridlines proportionally — never guess by eye alone.
  • Stacked bar trap: a middle segment's value = top minus bottom. Only the bottom segment can be read directly from the y-axis.
  • Line graph "fastest growing" means percentage growth, not absolute. The smallest line can be growing fastest in percentage terms.
  • Pie chart labels may show £ values instead of %. Always check — if they show values, calculate the total first, then derive percentages.
  • When combining data from multiple charts, verify they share common entities before calculating.
