Weighted Averages & Index Numbers

Two advanced topics that separate top-scoring candidates from the rest. Both appear when test designers want to catch people who grab the obvious average or the obvious percentage — and get it wrong.

1

Why Simple Averages Give Wrong Answers

A simple average treats every value equally. That works when the values represent equal-sized groups. It fails — silently and dangerously — when the groups are different sizes.

Imagine two investment funds. Fund A returned 12% and manages £80m. Fund B returned 8% and manages £120m. What's the overall portfolio return?

If you take a simple average — (12% + 8%) ÷ 2 = 10% — you're treating both funds as if they manage the same amount of money. They don't. Fund B manages 50% more capital, so its lower return should drag the overall average down below 10%, not sit right in the middle.

This is where weighted averages come in. The weight is the thing that makes one value "count more" than another — in this case, the capital managed.

Weighted Average Formula
Weighted Average = Σ(Value × Weight) ÷ Σ(Weight)
Sum of (each value multiplied by its weight), divided by the sum of all weights.
When to Suspect a Weighted Average

Any question that gives you individual averages or rates alongside different group sizes (headcount, capital, revenue, hours, population) is testing whether you'll weight correctly. If the question data includes both a rate/average and a size for each category, it's a weighted average question.

2

Weighted Averages — Portfolio Returns & Office Salaries

Two common test scenarios: portfolio returns weighted by capital, and average salaries weighted by headcount. Both work identically — multiply each value by its weight, sum the products, divide by total weight.

Worked Example 1 — Portfolio Returns An investment portfolio contains three funds. What is the overall weighted average return?
FundAnnual ReturnCapital (£m)
UK Equity Fund12%80
Global Bond Fund5%120
Property Fund9%50

Step 1: Multiply each return by its capital weight.

1UK Equity: 12% × £80m = 960
2Global Bond: 5% × £120m = 600
3Property: 9% × £50m = 450

Step 2: Sum the products and divide by total capital.

4Total weighted = 960 + 600 + 450 = 2,010
5Total capital = £80m + £120m + £50m = £250m
6Weighted average = 2,010 ÷ 250 = 8.04%

The simple average would be (12 + 5 + 9) ÷ 3 = 8.67%. The weighted answer (8.04%) is lower because the largest fund — Global Bond at £120m — has the lowest return, pulling the overall portfolio down.

✗ Simple Average

(12 + 5 + 9) ÷ 3 = 8.67%

Treats £80m, £120m, and £50m as if they're all the same size. They're not.

✓ Weighted Average

(960 + 600 + 450) ÷ 250 = 8.04%

The £120m bond fund's 5% return drags the portfolio below the simple midpoint.

Worked Example 2 — Average Salary by Office A consultancy has three offices. What is the firm-wide weighted average salary?
OfficeAvg SalaryHeadcount
London£68,000240
Manchester£52,000180
Edinburgh£48,00080

Step 1: Multiply each salary by headcount (= total salary bill per office).

1London: £68,000 × 240 = £16,320,000
2Manchester: £52,000 × 180 = £9,360,000
3Edinburgh: £48,000 × 80 = £3,840,000

Step 2: Sum total salaries and divide by total headcount.

4Total salary bill = £16,320,000 + £9,360,000 + £3,840,000 = £29,520,000
5Total headcount = 240 + 180 + 80 = 500
6Weighted average = £29,520,000 ÷ 500 = £59,040

The simple average would be (£68,000 + £52,000 + £48,000) ÷ 3 = £56,000. The weighted answer (£59,040) is higher because London — the highest-paid office — has the most staff, pulling the average up.

Quick Sanity Check

The weighted average must always fall between the smallest and largest individual values, and it will lean towards the group with the larger weight. If your answer falls outside the range of individual values, you've made an error.

3

Index Numbers — Reading Relative Change

An index expresses values relative to a base year. The base year is always set to 100. Every other value tells you how far that year has moved from the base — without revealing the actual underlying numbers.

Index 115 = 15% higher than the base. Index 92 = 8% lower than the base. Index 100 = unchanged from the base.

Tests use indices because they strip away the actual values, forcing you to think in relative terms. The trap comes when you need to calculate the change between two non-base years.

Worked Example 3 — UK Commercial Property Index Using the index below, answer three questions about property price movements.
Year20202021202220232024
Property Index100108115112120

Q1: What was the total percentage increase from 2020 to 2024?

1Base = 100, end = 120. Increase = 120 − 100 = 20. Percentage = 20 ÷ 100 × 100 = 20%

When the starting point is the base year, the maths is trivially easy — just read off the index value minus 100.

Q2: In which year did prices fall?

22021→2022: 108→115 (rose). 2022→2023: 115→112 (fell). 2023→2024: 112→120 (rose).
3Prices fell in 2023 (index dropped from 115 to 112).

Q3: If a property was worth £450,000 in 2020, what was its approximate value in 2024?

42024 index = 120, base = 100. Multiplier = 120 ÷ 100 = 1.20
5£450,000 × 1.20 = £540,000

To convert an index to an actual value: take the index value, divide by the base (100), and multiply by the known base-year amount. This gives you the proportional value at the target year.

Index to Real Value
Real Value = Base-Year Value × (Index ÷ 100)
Works for any year — just use that year's index number.
4

The Index Trap — Percentage Change Between Non-Base Years

This is where most candidates go wrong. When a question asks for the percentage change between two years, neither of which is the base year, you cannot simply subtract the index values and call it a percentage.

The index below is the same data from Worked Example 3. Watch what happens when we calculate the change from 2022 to 2024.

Year20202021202220232024
Property Index100108115112120
✗ Subtracting Index Values

"Change from 2022 to 2024 = 120 − 115 = 5%"

This treats the difference as a percentage. It's not — it's just a point difference. A 5-point change from 115 is not the same as a 5-point change from 100.

✓ Dividing by Starting Value

(120 − 115) ÷ 115 × 100 = 5 ÷ 115 = 4.35%

Divide by the STARTING year's index (115), not the base year (100). The base year is irrelevant here.

The Rule

Percentage change between two index values uses the same formula as any other percentage change: (New − Old) ÷ Old × 100. The "Old" value is the index at the starting year, not the base year. When both options appear in multiple choice — 5% and 4.35% — choose 4.35%.

Here's why the trap works so well: when the starting year is the base year, subtracting gives the correct answer (because dividing by 100 is the same as just reading the difference). Candidates learn this shortcut from the easy questions, then apply it to the hard question where the starting year isn't the base — and get caught.

From 2023 (112) to 2024 (120):

✗Wrong: 120 − 112 = "8%"
✓Correct: (120 − 112) ÷ 112 × 100 = 8 ÷ 112 × 100 = 7.14%

Practice — Weighted Averages & Index Numbers

Five questions targeting the core traps from this lesson. Each question includes its own data — read the table carefully before calculating.

Practice Question 1 — Weighted Average Return
A pension fund holds three asset classes. What is the fund's overall weighted average return?
Asset ClassAnnual ReturnAllocation (£m)
Equities11%150
Bonds4%200
Alternatives14%50

(A) 7.88%

(B) 8.50%

(C) 9.67%

(D) 10.25%

Show Answer ▾
1Equities: 11% × 150M = 16.5
2Bonds: 4% × 200M = 8.0
3Alternatives: 14% × 50M = 7.0
4Total weighted = 16.5 + 8.0 + 7.0 = 31.5
5Total allocation = 150 + 200 + 50 = 400
631.50 ÷ 400 × 100 = 7.88% → Answer: A

Trap: Option C (9.67%) is the simple average of the three returns: (11 + 4 + 14) ÷ 3 = 9.67%. This ignores the £200m bond allocation that drags the portfolio return down well below the midpoint of the three rates. Option B (8.50%) might tempt candidates who weight by equal thirds instead of by capital.

Practice Question 2 — Weighted Average Salary
A technology firm has staff across three departments. What is the company's overall average salary?
DepartmentAverage SalaryHeadcount
Engineering£72,000120
Sales£55,00080
Operations£42,000200

(A) £53,600

(B) £56,333

(C) £54,800

(D) £58,100

Show Answer ▾
1Engineering: £72,000 × 120 = £8,640,000
2Sales: £55,000 × 80 = £4,400,000
3Operations: £42,000 × 200 = £8,400,000
4Total salary bill = £8,640,000 + £4,400,000 + £8,400,000 = £21,440,000
5Total headcount = 120 + 80 + 200 = 400
6£21,440,000 ÷ 400 = £53,600 → Answer: A

Trap: Option B (£56,333) is the simple average of the three department salaries: (£72,000 + £55,000 + £42,000) ÷ 3 = £56,333. Operations has the most staff (200 out of 400) and the lowest salary, pulling the weighted average down to £53,600.

The table below shows a Consumer Electronics Price Index. If a standard laptop cost £850 in 2021, what would the same specification cost in 2025?
Year20212022202320242025
Electronics Index10096918882

(A) £697

(B) £718

(C) £748

(D) £782

Show Answer ▾

The base year (2021) = 100, and the laptop cost £850 in the base year. The 2025 index = 82.

1Multiplier = 82 ÷ 100 = 0.82
2£850 × 0.82 = £697 → Answer: A

This index falls over time (electronics get cheaper). The 2025 value of 82 means prices are 18% lower than the base year. Practice Question 4 — Index Percentage Change (Non-Base Year)
Using the Regional Office Rental Index below, what was the percentage change in rental costs from 2022 to 2025?
Year202020212022202320242025
Rental Index100105112118124130

(A) 16.07%

(B) 18.00%

(C) 18.75%

(D) 30.00%

Show Answer ▾

2022 index = 112. 2025 index = 130. The starting value is 112 (not the base of 100).

1(130 − 112) ÷ 112 × 100 = 18 ÷ 112 × 100 = 16.07% → Answer: A

Trap: Option B (18.00%) comes from subtracting the index values (130 − 112 = 18) and treating that as a percentage. Option D (30.00%) is the change from the base year (2020 to 2025), answering the wrong time period entirely.

Practice Question 5 — Combined Weighted Average + Comparison (Hard)
A retail group operates three store formats. Using the data below, by how many percentage points does the simple average profit margin exceed the weighted average profit margin?
FormatProfit MarginRevenue (£m)
Superstore3.0%400
Express7.5%200
Online1.5%200

(A) 0.25 percentage points

(B) 0.50 percentage points

(C) 0.75 percentage points

(D) 1.00 percentage points

Show Answer ▾

Simple average:

1(3.0 + 7.5 + 1.5) ÷ 3 = 12.0 ÷ 3 = 4.00%

Weighted average (weighted by revenue):

2Superstore: 3.0% × 400 = 12.0
3Express: 7.5% × 200 = 15.0
4Online: 1.5% × 200 = 3.0
5Total weighted = 12.0 + 15.0 + 3.0 = 30.0
6Total revenue = 400 + 200 + 200 = 800
7Weighted average = 30.0 ÷ 800 × 100 = 3.75%

Difference:

84.00% − 3.75% = 0.25 percentage points → Answer: A

The weighted average is lower than the simple average because Superstore (the largest revenue contributor at £400m) has a below-average margin of just 3.0%, and Online (£200m) is even lower at 1.5%. Together they represent 75% of total revenue but have the two weakest margins. Express has the best margin (7.5%) but only generates £200m — its high profitability rate is partially diluted by Superstore's dominance. The simple average gives each format equal voice regardless of size; the weighted average reflects economic reality.

Key Takeaways

  • Use a weighted average whenever the question gives you individual rates or averages alongside different group sizes. The giveaway: each category has both a value (return, salary, margin) and a size (capital, headcount, revenue).
  • The weighted average always falls between the smallest and largest individual values, leaning towards the group with the largest weight. If your answer falls outside that range, you've made an error.
  • Index numbers express values relative to a base year (= 100). To convert an index to a real value: multiply the base-year amount by the index divided by 100.
  • Percentage change between non-base years uses the standard formula: (New − Old) ÷ Old × 100. Divide by the starting year's index, not the base year. The difference between index points and actual percentages is the single most common trap.
  • If two answer options in an index question are suspiciously close — like 18% and 16.1% — the smaller one is almost certainly correct, because the candidate who gets 18% forgot to divide by the starting index.
