Module 3 · Lesson 3.1

Reading Tables & Extracting Data

In a real aptitude test, the hardest part is rarely the maths — it's finding the right numbers in a complex table under time pressure. This lesson teaches you how.

1

The Real Challenge

Module 2 gave you the calculation skills. You can find percentages, work with ratios, convert currencies, and estimate under pressure. Now comes the part that actually separates high scorers from everyone else: extracting the right data from complex tables before you calculate.

In SHL and Aon tests, the data is deliberately designed to be dense. Tables have multiple rows, multiple columns, mixed units, footnotes, and column headers that look similar but mean different things. The calculation itself is often straightforward — a single percentage or subtraction. The difficulty is making sure you're calculating with the right numbers.

The Data Interpretation Equation
Right Data + Right Calculation = Right Answer
Wrong Data + Right Calculation = Wrong Answer (that matches a trap option)
Most wrong answers in data interpretation come from extracting the wrong number, not from calculating incorrectly.

This lesson teaches four skills in order of increasing difficulty: simple table reading, handling mixed units, navigating multi-variable tables, and distinguishing cumulative from individual data. Each one addresses a specific trap that test designers build into their questions.

2

Skill 1 — Simple Tables & the Highlight Method

The highlight method is simple: trace the row with one finger and the column with another until they meet. On screen, use a pen or your cursor. This sounds obvious, but under time pressure, candidates routinely read the wrong row or the wrong column — especially in tables with 5+ rows and 4+ columns where the data is tightly packed.

Table 1 — Quarterly Revenue by Region (£ thousands)

Region Q1 Q2 Q3 Q4
North 2,650 2,840 3,120 2,980
South 3,410 3,680 3,950 4,120
East 1,890 2,150 2,340 2,510
West 2,240 2,470 2,680 2,890
Source: Regional Sales Report FY2024. All figures in £ thousands.
Worked Example 1 — Single Cell "What was the North region's revenue in Q2?"
1Trace row: Find "North" in the left column
2Trace column: Find "Q2" in the header
3Intersection: North row × Q2 column = 2,840
4Check units: Table says "£ thousands" → Answer: £2,840,000

The gold-highlighted cell in the table above shows the intersection. In a real test, you do this mentally — but always check the column header AND the row label before writing down the number.

Worked Example 2 — Row Aggregation "What was the total revenue across all regions in Q3?"
1Identify: "All regions" = sum the entire Q3 column
2North Q3: £3,120k
3South Q3: £3,950k
4East Q3: £2,340k
5West Q3: £2,680k
6Total: £3,120 + £3,950 + £2,340 + £2,680 = £12,090k = £12,090,000

Trap: Reading the Q4 column instead of Q3 — the numbers are close enough that you might not notice the error, and a wrong answer option will match.

Speed Tip

When summing a column, enter the values into your calculator without rounding. Type each number as you trace down the column — don't try to hold four numbers in your head before starting the addition. One misremembered digit costs you 30 seconds of recalculation.

3

Skill 2 — Tables with Mixed Units

This is the most common trap in data interpretation tests. The table header says one unit (e.g., "£ thousands"), but the question asks for the answer in a different unit (e.g., "£ millions"), or — worse — different columns within the same table use different units. Test designers do this deliberately. Always read the column header and any footnotes before extracting a single number.

Table 2 — BrightPath Consulting: Annual Financial Summary

Metric 2022 2023 2024
Revenue (£m) 3.2 3.5 3.8
Operating Costs (£k) 2,080 2,275 2,450
Employees 84 91 98
Client Satisfaction (%) 87 89 92
Revenue in £ millions. Operating costs in £ thousands. Satisfaction scores from annual client survey.
Worked Example 3 — Mixed Units "What was BrightPath's operating profit margin in 2024?"

First: Standardise the units.

1Revenue 2024: £3.8m = £3,800,000
2Costs 2024: £2,450k = £2,450,000

Now calculate:

3Profit: £3,800,000 − £2,450,000 = £1,350,000
4Margin: £1,350,000 ÷ £3,800,000 × 100 = 35.5%
Without Unit Check

Revenue: 3.8

Costs: 2,450

"Profit": 3.8 − 2,450 = −2,446.2

−2,446.2 (?)
Mixing £m and £k without converting. The result is nonsensical — costs appear 644× larger than revenue. Sanity check catches this, but it costs you 30+ seconds.
With Unit Check

Revenue: £3.8m = £3,800k

Costs: £2,450k

Profit: £3,800k − £2,450k = £1,350k

Margin: 35.5%
Convert both to the same unit (£k or £m) before calculating. Revenue > costs — profit is positive. Margin is a realistic ~35%. Makes sense.
The Three Places Units Hide

1. Column headers — "Revenue (£m)" vs "Costs (£k)" in the same table. 2. Table footnotes — "All figures in £ thousands unless stated otherwise." 3. The question itself — "Express your answer in £ millions" when the table is in £ thousands. Check all three before calculating.

4

Skill 3 — Multi-Variable Tables

Real test tables often have three or more variables — for example, headcount broken down by office, department, and seniority level. The challenge is navigating multiple dimensions simultaneously without losing your place. The approach is the same as the highlight method, but with an extra step: identify which variable the question is asking about before you look at the table.

Table 3 — Headcount by Office, Department & Seniority

Office Department Junior Mid-Level Senior
London Audit 65 38 18
Advisory 48 31 14
Tax 32 19 10
Manchester Audit 42 25 12
Advisory 30 18 8
Tax 22 14 8
Edinburgh Audit 38 22 15
Advisory 28 16 12
Tax 18 12 8
Worked Example 4 — Multi-Variable Navigation "What percentage of all senior employees across the firm are based in London?"

Step 1: Identify what the question asks

We need: (London senior total) ÷ (All offices senior total) × 100.

Step 2: Extract London seniors

1London Audit Senior: 18
2London Advisory Senior: 14
3London Tax Senior: 10
4London Senior Total: 18 + 14 + 10 = 42

Step 3: Extract all seniors

5Manchester Seniors: 12 + 8 + 8 = 28
6Edinburgh Seniors: 15 + 12 + 8 = 35
7All Seniors: 42 + 28 + 35 = 105

Step 4: Calculate

842 ÷ 105 × 100 = 40.0%

Trap: Counting only one London department (e.g., just Audit) and dividing by total seniors — gives 18/105 = 17.1%, which will likely appear as a wrong answer option.

Multi-Variable Navigation Rule

Before touching the table, mentally note exactly what you need: "I need [VARIABLE 1] filtered by [VARIABLE 2], compared to [VARIABLE 3]." In the example above: "I need SENIOR count filtered by LONDON, compared to ALL OFFICES." This prevents you from accidentally summing the wrong dimension.

5

Skill 4 — Cumulative vs Individual Data

Some tables show cumulative (year-to-date) figures rather than individual monthly or quarterly values. This is the single most dangerous trap in table reading because the numbers look exactly like individual figures — there's nothing visually different about them. The only clue is in the column header or footnote.

Table 4 — Monthly Sales: Cumulative Year-to-Date (£ thousands)

Month Product A (YTD) Product B (YTD) Total (YTD)
January 580 400 980
February 1,290 890 2,180
March 2,150 1,280 3,430
April 2,880 1,640 4,520
YTD = Year-to-date cumulative totals. All figures in £ thousands.
Worked Example 5 — Cumulative Trap "How much did Product A sell in February alone?"
✗Wrong: Reading the February row directly → £1,290k. This is the cumulative total from January through February, not February alone.
✓Correct: February individual = February YTD − January YTD
1February YTD: £1,290k
2January YTD: £580k
3February individual: £1,290k − £580k = £710k

The cumulative trap is especially effective because £1,290k looks like a perfectly reasonable February sales figure. You'd never suspect it was wrong unless you noticed "YTD" in the column header.

The Cumulative Rule
Individual Month = Current Month (YTD) − Previous Month (YTD)
Always check column headers for "YTD", "Cumulative", or "Running Total" before extracting any value.
How to Spot Cumulative Tables

Three giveaways: 1. Values only increase as you go down the rows (real monthly sales would fluctuate). 2. The header contains "YTD", "Cumulative", or "Running Total". 3. The first row's value equals what you'd expect for a single period, but later rows are suspiciously large. If you see any of these signals, assume cumulative until proven otherwise.

Practice — Data Extraction

Answer the following questions. For each one, identify which table you need, what data to extract, and any unit or cumulative traps before calculating.

Practice Question 1
What was the firm's total revenue across all service lines in 2024? (Express your answer in £ millions.)
Table 5 — Revenue by Service Line (£ thousands)
Service Line 2022 2023 2024
Audit 4,380 4,620 4,850
Advisory 3,250 3,540 3,920
Consulting 2,180 2,490 2,680
Tax 1,520 1,610 1,750
All figures in £ thousands.

(A) £11.33m

(B) £12.26m

(C) £13.20m

(D) £15.16m

Show Answer ▾

Table: Table 5. Column: 2024. Unit check: Table is in £ thousands; question asks for £ millions.

1£4,850 + £3,920 + £2,680 + £1,750 = £13,200 (in £k)
2Convert: £13,200k ÷ 1,000 = £13.20m → Answer: C

Trap: Option B (£12.26m) is the 2023 total. Option D (£15.16m) sums all three years of one service line. Always check you're reading the right column.

Practice Question 2
Which service line had the highest proportion of its annual FY2024 revenue generated in Q3?
Table 6 — Quarterly Revenue Breakdown, FY2024 (£ thousands)
Service Line Q1 Q2 Q3 Q4 Total
Audit 1,180 1,210 1,250 1,210 4,850
Advisory 880 960 1,050 1,030 3,920
Consulting 520 610 740 810 2,680
Tax 420 430 440 460 1,750

(A) Audit

(B) Advisory

(C) Consulting

(D) Tax

Show Answer ▾

Method: Calculate Q3 ÷ Total for each service line.

1Audit: 1,250 ÷ 4,850 = 25.8%
2Advisory: 1,050 ÷ 3,920 = 26.8%
3Consulting: 740 ÷ 2,680 = 27.6%
4Tax: 440 ÷ 1,750 = 25.1%

Answer: C — Consulting had the highest Q3 concentration at 27.6%.

Trap: Consulting has the smallest absolute Q3 revenue (£740k), so picking the largest absolute number (Audit at £1,250k) would give the wrong answer. The question asks about proportion, not absolute value.

Practice Question 4
What was the total sales revenue for March alone?
Table 4 — Monthly Sales: Cumulative Year-to-Date (£ thousands)
Month Product A (YTD) Product B (YTD) Total (YTD)
January 580 400 980
February 1,290 890 2,180
March 2,150 1,280 3,430
April 2,880 1,640 4,520
YTD = Year-to-date cumulative totals. All figures in £ thousands.

(A) £1,250k

(B) £1,590k

(C) £2,150k

(D) £3,430k

Show Answer ▾

Cumulative table! March individual = March YTD − February YTD.

1March Total (YTD): £3,430k
2February Total (YTD): £2,180k (not February alone — this is also cumulative)
3March individual: £3,430 − £2,180 = £1,250k

Wait — but we also need to check. The "Total" column YTD values:

4March YTD Total: £3,430k. Feb YTD Total: £2,180k.
5March individual total: £3,430 − £2,180 = £1,250k → Answer: A

Trap: Option D (£3,430k) is the cumulative March value read directly — the classic cumulative trap. Option C (£2,150k) is Product A's March YTD, not the total. Option B (£1,590k) is what you get from an incorrect subtraction.

Practice Question 5
Which service line showed the largest percentage growth from 2023 to 2024?
Table 5 — Revenue by Service Line (£ thousands)
Service Line 2022 2023 2024
Audit 4,380 4,620 4,850
Advisory 3,250 3,540 3,920
Consulting 2,180 2,490 2,680
Tax 1,520 1,610 1,750
All figures in £ thousands.

(A) Audit (5.0%)

(B) Advisory (10.7%)

(C) Consulting (7.6%)

(D) Tax (8.7%)

Show Answer ▾

Method: % change = (2024 − 2023) ÷ 2023 × 100. Divide by OLD value (2023).

1Audit: (4,850 − 4,620) ÷ 4,620 = 230 ÷ 4,620 = 5.0%
2Advisory: (3,920 − 3,540) ÷ 3,540 = 380 ÷ 3,540 = 10.7%
3Consulting: (2,680 − 2,490) ÷ 2,490 = 190 ÷ 2,490 = 7.6%
4Tax: (1,750 − 1,610) ÷ 1,610 = 140 ÷ 1,610 = 8.7%

Answer: B — Advisory at 10.7%

Trap: Audit has the largest absolute growth (£230k vs Advisory's £380k — actually Advisory wins on both). But in other questions, the largest absolute change and the largest percentage change might differ. Always check which the question asks for.

Key Takeaways

  • Use the highlight method: trace the row and column to their intersection before writing down any number. Under pressure, reading the wrong row or column is the most common error.
  • Check units in three places before calculating: column headers, table footnotes, and the question itself. Mixed units (£m and £k in the same table) are a deliberate trap.
  • For multi-variable tables, write down exactly what you need before looking at the data: "I need [variable] filtered by [condition], compared to [total]."
  • Cumulative (YTD) tables are the most dangerous trap. Individual period value = Current YTD − Previous YTD. Look for "YTD", "Cumulative", or steadily increasing values.
  • When summing a column, enter each number into your calculator as you trace down — don't try to memorise four numbers and add them afterwards.
