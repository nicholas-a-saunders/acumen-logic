Profit, Loss & Financial Statements

This is not accounting. You won't see debits, credits, or balance sheets. What you will see — in almost every finance-sector test — is simplified P&L data that demands fast, accurate calculation of revenue, costs, profit, and margins.

1

The P&L Vocabulary You Need

Aptitude tests for Goldman Sachs, KPMG, and McKinsey don't test accounting knowledge. They test whether you can extract numbers from simplified financial data and calculate relationships between them. You need exactly six terms:

Revenue (Sales / Turnover)
Total income before any costs are deducted. The top line. All questions start here.
Cost of Sales (COGS / Direct Costs)
Costs directly tied to delivering the product or service. Materials, labour, production.
Gross Profit
Revenue minus cost of sales. Shows profitability before overheads. = Revenue − COGS.
Operating Expenses (Overheads)
Rent, salaries, marketing, admin — costs not directly tied to production.
Operating Profit (EBIT)
Gross profit minus operating expenses. The core measure of business performance.
Net Profit
Operating profit minus tax and interest. The bottom line — what the business actually keeps.
Watch for Synonym Traps

Tests use these terms interchangeably to confuse: Revenue = Sales = Turnover. Cost of Sales = COGS = Direct Costs. Operating Profit = EBIT. If the question says "turnover" and the table says "revenue", they mean the same thing. Don't waste time looking for a different row.

The P&L Cascade
Revenue → − COGS = Gross Profit → − Overheads = Operating Profit → − Tax & Interest = Net Profit
Each level subtracts a different category of costs. Tests can ask about any level.

3

Margins — The Most Tested P&L Skill

A margin converts a profit figure into a percentage of revenue. Tests love margins because the formula is simple but the trap — dividing by the wrong base — catches candidates every time. Three margins appear in tests:

Gross Margin
Gross Margin = Gross Profit ÷ Revenue × 100
Operating Margin
Operating Margin = Operating Profit ÷ Revenue × 100
Net Margin
Net Margin = Net Profit ÷ Revenue × 100

The denominator is always revenue. Not cost. Not total expenses. Not gross profit. Every margin divides by revenue — the top line. This is the single rule you need.

Using the Meridian data above:

Worked Example 1 Calculate Meridian Consulting's gross margin, operating margin, and net margin for FY2024.
1Gross margin = £5,040k ÷ £8,400k × 100 = 60.0%
2Operating margin = £2,100k ÷ £8,400k × 100 = 25.0%
3Net margin = £1,417.5k ÷ £8,400k × 100 = 16.9%

Quick sense-check: margins should decrease as you move down the P&L (gross > operating > net). If your operating margin is higher than your gross margin, you've made an error.

✗ Margin on Cost

£5,040k ÷ £3,360k = 150%

This is gross profit as a percentage of cost — a markup, not a margin. Tests include this as a trap option.

✓ Margin on Revenue

£5,040k ÷ £8,400k = 60.0%

Margin always divides by revenue. Markup divides by cost. If you see an answer above 100%, you've calculated markup.

Margin vs Markup

Margin = profit ÷ revenue. Markup = profit ÷ cost. They use the same numerator but different denominators. If a question asks for "margin" and your answer is over 100%, you've divided by cost instead of revenue. In SHL tests, both values will appear as answer options.

Divisional P&L — Where Tests Get Harder

Real test data rarely shows a single company P&L. More commonly, you'll see a divisional breakdown — multiple business units with different revenue and cost structures. This forces you to extract the right division's data before calculating. Here's a realistic example:

Worked Example 2 Which division has the highest gross margin? And which has the highest operating margin? Are they the same division?

Gross margins (gross profit ÷ revenue):

1Strategy: £2,340k ÷ £3,600k = 65.0%
2Technology: £1,540k ÷ £2,800k = 55.0%
3Operations: £1,160k ÷ £2,000k = 58.0%

Operating margins (operating profit ÷ revenue):

4Strategy: £1,260k ÷ £3,600k = 35.0%
5Technology: £504k ÷ £2,800k = 18.0%
6Operations: £336k ÷ £2,000k = 16.8%

Strategy has both the highest gross margin (65.0%) and the highest operating margin (35.0%).

But notice: Operations has a higher gross margin (58.0%) than Technology (55.0%), yet a lower operating margin (16.8% vs 18.0%). This happens because Operations has proportionally higher overheads. The ranking can flip between profit levels — tests exploit this by asking about one margin and including the other margin's ranking as a trap.

5

Comparing Periods — Revenue Up, Profit Down

The most challenging P&L questions give you two time periods and ask you to explain the change. The key insight: revenue can grow while profit falls if costs grow faster. Tests love this because it catches candidates who assume "bigger revenue = bigger profit".

Source C Meridian Consulting — Year-on-Year Comparison (£ thousands)
Line ItemFY2023FY2024Change
Revenue7,2008,400+16.7%
Cost of Sales(2,520)(3,360)+33.3%
Gross Profit4,6805,040+7.7%
Operating Expenses(2,520)(2,940)+16.7%
Operating Profit2,1602,100−2.8%

Revenue grew 16.7% but operating profit fell 2.8%. Why? Because cost of sales grew at double the rate of revenue (33.3% vs 16.7%), compressing the gross margin from 65.0% to 60.0%. Operating expenses grew in line with revenue, but the damage was already done at the gross profit level.

In a test, this data generates questions like "Which cost category grew faster than revenue?" and "What was the change in gross margin from FY2023 to FY2024?" — both require understanding the P&L structure, not just individual calculations.

The Margin Compression Signal

When a question says "revenue increased but operating profit decreased", immediately check whether costs grew faster than revenue. Calculate both growth rates and compare them. The cost category that outpaced revenue is where the margin compression happened.

Key Takeaways

  • The P&L is a cascade: Revenue → Gross Profit → Operating Profit → Net Profit. Each level subtracts a different cost category. Know the sequence so you don't confuse what's been subtracted at each stage.
  • Margins always divide by revenue. If your answer is above 100%, you've divided by cost (that's markup, not margin). This is the most common P&L error in aptitude tests.
  • Revenue, Sales, and Turnover mean the same thing. Cost of Sales, COGS, and Direct Costs mean the same thing. Don't panic if the table uses different terminology from the question.
  • When comparing periods, check whether costs grew faster than revenue. Rising revenue with falling profit means a cost category outpaced growth — find which one.
  • Percentage point change and percentage change are different. A margin moving from 65% to 60% is a 5 percentage point decrease but a 7.7% decrease. Tests always include both as answer options.
  • In divisional P&Ls, the division with the highest gross margin may not have the highest operating margin. Overheads can flip the rankings — always calculate the specific margin the question asks about.
