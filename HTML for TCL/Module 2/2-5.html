Module 2 · Lesson 2.5

Speed Arithmetic & Estimation

You have a calculator. The bottleneck isn't arithmetic — it's everything around the arithmetic. This lesson is about deciding faster, not calculating faster.

1

Where Your Time Actually Goes

An SHL numerical reasoning test gives you approximately 83 seconds per question. Most candidates assume the calculation eats most of that time. It doesn't. Here's the real breakdown — including the hidden time sink nobody talks about.

Read
~20s
Decide
~15s
Calculate
~25s
Check
~10s
Wasted
~13s
Read question (20s) Decide what to calculate (15s) Calculator work (25s) Sanity check (10s) Wasted — hesitation, re-reads, restarts (13s)

The red bar is where you lose. Those 13 seconds aren't spent calculating — they're spent hesitating. Re-reading the question because you're unsure what they want. Staring at the data table deciding which numbers to use. Starting a calculation, realising it's wrong, and restarting.

With a calculator, the arithmetic takes 25 seconds regardless of the numbers. You can't meaningfully speed that up. But you can eliminate the 13 seconds of waste — and that's worth two extra questions in a 25-question test.

Every technique in this lesson targets the time around the calculation, not the calculation itself.

2

Read Before You Calculate

The single biggest time killer is calculating the wrong thing. You crunch the numbers perfectly, get an answer that matches one of the options, select it — and it's wrong because you answered a different question to the one being asked. That costs you 60–90 seconds: the original attempt plus the restart.

The fix takes 10 seconds. Before touching your calculator, answer three questions.

The 10-Second Pre-Check
1. What FORMAT do they want? (%, £, ratio, units?)
2. What is the BASE? (of revenue? of cost? of total?)
3. How many STEPS will this take?
10 seconds upfront saves 60+ seconds of wrong-answer restarts.
Pre-Check in Action "Operating costs were 62% of revenue in Q1 and 58% of revenue in Q2. Revenue was £340k in Q1 and £425k in Q2. What was the percentage change in profit between Q1 and Q2?"

Pre-check (10 seconds):

1FORMAT: Percentage change (not £ profit, not a ratio)
2BASE: Percentage change of profit — divide by Q1 profit (the OLD value)
3STEPS: Four — calculate Q1 profit, Q2 profit, find difference, divide by Q1 profit

Now calculate:

4Q1 profit: £340k × (1 − 0.62) = £340k × 0.38 = £129,200
5Q2 profit: £425k × (1 − 0.58) = £425k × 0.42 = £178,500
6Change: (£178,500 − £129,200) ÷ £129,200 × 100 = 38.2%
What Goes Wrong Without the Pre-Check

Without step 1, you might calculate £ profit instead of % change — correct number, wrong format. Without step 2, you might divide by Q2 profit instead of Q1 — the reverse percentage trap. Without step 3, you might calculate Q1 profit and immediately try to jump to the answer, forgetting you need Q2 as well. All three errors produce answers that appear in the options. The pre-check prevents all of them.

3

Question Triage — The 5-Second Classification

Not all questions demand equal time. Elite scorers classify every question within 5 seconds of reading it, then allocate time accordingly. The goal isn't perfection — it's maximum correct answers across the entire test.

TierStepsTime BudgetStrategy
Tier 11 step~40 secondsExecute immediately. Bank the spare time.
Tier 22–3 steps~75 secondsStandard execution. Store intermediate results in calculator memory.
Tier 34+ steps~90 secondsRead twice. If you immediately see the path forward, proceed with caution. If confused after two reads, mark and move on — return if time permits.
The 45-Second Rule
If you've spent 45 seconds and aren't close to an answer — stop, guess, move on.
One abandoned question costs you ~4 points. Running out of time costs you every unanswered question.
Triage in Action You see these three questions in sequence. How do you allocate your time?

Q12: "What is 15% of £240,000?"

→Tier 1. One calculation. Budget: 40s. You'll finish in 20s and bank 20s.

Q13: "Revenue rose from £550k to £680k. Costs rose from £340k to £425k. What was the percentage change in profit margin?"

→Tier 3. Four steps minimum (two margins, difference, % change). Budget: 90s. Read carefully — if the path is clear, proceed; if not, mark and return.

Q14: "A workforce of 1,200 is reduced by 8%. How many remain?"

→Tier 1. One multiplication. Budget: 40s. Finish in 15s, bank 25s.

By triaging, you do Q12 and Q14 first (35 seconds total), bank 45 spare seconds, then tackle Q13 with a combined budget of 135 seconds instead of 83.

How to Count Steps Instantly

Count the number of intermediate values you need before you can reach the answer. "15% of £240k" needs zero intermediates — one step. "Percentage change in profit margin" needs Q1 margin, Q2 margin, and a percentage change calculation — that's three intermediates, so four steps total. If you count 3+ intermediates, it's Tier 3.

4

Estimation — When Exact Answers Are Overkill

In a multiple-choice test, you sometimes don't need the exact answer. If the options are spread far enough apart, a rough estimate eliminates three of four options in seconds. Glance at the options before calculating. If they're far apart, estimate. If they're clustered, calculate precisely.

Estimation Saves 20 Seconds What is 23% of £648,000?

(A) £124,000

(B) £149,040

(C) £186,000

(D) £212,000

1Options are spread apart. Estimate: ~25% of ~£650k ≈ £162,500
2Actual is slightly less (23%, not 25%) → ~£149k
3Only B (£149,040) is close → Answer: B

No calculator needed. ~8 seconds vs ~25 seconds for precise arithmetic.

Options Clustered — Must Be Precise

(A) £58,200

(B) £61,200

(C) £63,800

(D) £68,000

Use calculator
Options are within ~15% of each other. An estimate won't reliably distinguish between A, B, and C. Calculate precisely.
Options Spread — Estimate

(A) £124,000

(B) £149,040

(C) £186,000

(D) £212,000

Estimate
Options are 20–40% apart. A rough estimate clearly points to one answer. Don't waste time on precision.
5

Calculator Discipline

Your calculator gives you the right answer to whatever you type in. The problem is that what you type in is often wrong. Three errors account for almost every calculator mistake in timed tests.

The Three Calculator Killers
1. Decimal placement    2. Unit mismatch    3. Stale display
Each costs 30–60 seconds when you have to identify the error and recalculate.
Killer 1 — Decimal Placement "Operating costs are 68% of revenue (£2.4m)." You type 2,400,000 × 6.8 instead of × 0.68.
✗2,400,000 × 6.8 = 16,320,000 — costs are 7× revenue. Impossible.
✓2,400,000 × 0.68 = 1,632,000

Fix: always convert percentages to decimals before you touch the calculator. 68% → type 0.68 directly.

Killer 2 — Unit Mismatch The data table shows revenue in £ millions, but one column is in £ thousands. You calculate without noticing.
✗Revenue £2.4m minus costs "£680" → reads as £2,400,000 − £680 = £2,399,320
✓Costs are £680k = £680,000 → £2,400,000 − £680,000 = £1,720,000

Fix: before calculating, check the column headers and footnotes for units. If mixed, standardise everything to one unit first.

Killer 3 — Stale Display You calculated £340,000 for the previous step. Now you need 15% of £240,000, but you accidentally multiply the old result.
✗340,000 × 0.15 = 51,000 — wrong base number.
✓Clear → 240,000 × 0.15 = 36,000

Fix: for multi-step problems, store intermediate values in calculator memory (STO function) rather than keeping them on display. This prevents stale display errors and keeps your working organised.

The Memory Habit (STO Function)

Use your calculator's memory (STO) to store intermediate results, not just the display. For multi-step problems: Revenue → STO A, Costs → STO B, then recall with RCL A or RCL B when needed. This is faster than retyping and eliminates stale display errors. Create a simple mental map: A always = Revenue, B always = Costs, C always = Profit, etc. Three seconds of storage saves thirty seconds of recalculation.

6

Sanity Checking — The 5-Second Safety Net

Before selecting your answer, spend 5 seconds asking: "Does this number make sense?" This single habit catches decimal errors, direction errors, and unit errors — the three mistakes that account for the vast majority of wrong answers from candidates who know the maths.

Three Instant Sanity Checks
1. Order of magnitude    2. Direction    3. Reasonableness
5 seconds. Catches 80% of errors. Non-negotiable on every question.
Check 1 — Order of Magnitude You're calculating 15% of £240,000 and get £360,000.

Sanity check: 15% of something must be smaller than the original. £360,000 > £240,000. Impossible. You've multiplied by 1.5 instead of 0.15.

✓£240,000 × 0.15 = £36,000
Check 2 — Direction Revenue fell by 12%, and you've calculated the new revenue as higher than the original.

Sanity check: "Fell" means the new value must be lower. If your answer is higher, you've multiplied by 1.12 instead of 0.88. Five-second catch.

Check 3 — Reasonableness You've calculated that a mid-level employee's annual salary is £450,000, or that a company's profit margin is 340%.

Sanity check: Does this pass the smell test? A mid-level salary of £450k is almost certainly a decimal error (probably £45,000). A 340% margin is virtually impossible in any real business — likely a unit or base error. Trust your instincts.

7

Practice — Strategy Application

For each question below, apply the strategies from this lesson: pre-check (format, base, steps), triage (tier classification), and decide whether to estimate or calculate precisely.

Practice Question 1
A company's revenue is £3.6m. Operating costs are 72% of revenue. What is the profit?

(A) £1,008,000

(B) £1,260,000

(C) £2,592,000

(D) £3,200,000

Show Answer ▾

Pre-check: Format = £. Base = revenue. Steps = 1 (profit = revenue × (1 − 0.72)).

Triage: Tier 1. One step.

Estimate or precise? Options are spread. Estimate: ~30% of ~£3.6m ≈ £1.08m. Only A is close.

1£3,600,000 × 0.28 = £1,008,000 → Answer: A

Trap: Option C (£2,592,000) is 72% of revenue — the costs, not the profit. The pre-check ("what is the profit?") prevents this.

Practice Question 2
Revenue grew from £1.8m to £2.34m. What was the percentage increase?

(A) 23%

(B) 30%

(C) 35%

(D) 77%

Show Answer ▾

Pre-check: Format = %. Base = OLD revenue (£1.8m). Steps = 2 (difference, then divide by old).

Triage: Tier 1–2. Two sub-calculations but straightforward.

1Difference: £2.34m − £1.8m = £0.54m
2% change: £0.54m ÷ £1.8m × 100 = 30% → Answer: B

Trap: Option A (23%) is what you get dividing by the NEW value (£0.54m ÷ £2.34m = 23.1%). Always divide by the OLD value. Option D (77%) is £1.8m ÷ £2.34m — completely wrong operation.

Practice Question 3
Classify this question as Tier 1, 2, or 3 — then decide your approach: "In 2023, Company A had revenue of £4.2m (profit margin 18%) and Company B had revenue of £3.1m (profit margin 24%). What was the combined profit of both companies, and which company contributed the greater share?"
Show Answer ▾

Triage: Tier 3. You need: A's profit, B's profit, combined total, and a comparison. That's 4 intermediate values.

Strategy: If you're ahead on time, attempt it. If you're behind, mark and return. If you attempt it, use calculator memory (STO) for each profit figure.

1A's profit: £4,200,000 × 0.18 = £756,000 → STO A
2B's profit: £3,100,000 × 0.24 = £744,000 → STO B
3Combined: RCL A + RCL B = £1,500,000
4Company A contributes more (£756k vs £744k) — despite lower margin, higher revenue wins.
Practice Question 4
You calculate that a 12% decrease on £850,000 gives £952,000. What went wrong?
Show Answer ▾

Sanity check — Direction: A 12% decrease must produce a number smaller than £850,000. £952,000 is larger. You multiplied by 1.12 (increase) instead of 0.88 (decrease).

✓£850,000 × 0.88 = £748,000

This is exactly the kind of error that a 5-second sanity check catches before you commit to the wrong answer.

Practice Question 5
After a 20% price increase, a product costs £54. What was the original price?

(A) £43.20

(B) £45.00

(C) £46.80

(D) £64.80

Show Answer ▾

Pre-check: Format = £. This is a reverse percentage — they give you the result and ask for the original. Divide by the multiplier, don't subtract 20%.

1£54 ÷ 1.20 = £45.00 → Answer: B

Trap: Option A (£43.20) is £54 − 20% of £54 = £54 − £10.80 — the classic reverse percentage error. Option D (£64.80) is £54 × 1.20 — wrong direction entirely. The pre-check ("they give me the result, I need the original") prevents both traps.

Practice Question 6
A data table shows costs in £ thousands and revenue in £ millions. Revenue = £4.2m. Costs = £2,940 (in £k). What is the profit margin?

(A) 30%

(B) 42%

(C) 58%

(D) 70%

Show Answer ▾

Pre-check: Format = %. Base = revenue (profit margin is always profit ÷ revenue). Unit check: costs are in £k → £2,940k = £2,940,000.

1Profit: £4,200,000 − £2,940,000 = £1,260,000
2Margin: £1,260,000 ÷ £4,200,000 × 100 = 30% → Answer: A

Trap: If you miss the unit mismatch and treat costs as £2,940, you get profit of £4,197,060 and a margin of 99.9% — which isn't even an option. If your answer isn't among the options, check units first.

Key Takeaways

  • Your calculator handles the arithmetic. The bottleneck is deciding what to calculate — eliminate hesitation with the 10-second pre-check (format, base, steps).
  • Triage every question in 5 seconds: Tier 1 (one step — execute fast), Tier 2 (two to three steps — standard time), Tier 3 (four+ steps — read twice, proceed only if the path is clear).
  • The 45-second rule: if you're not close to an answer after 45 seconds, make your best guess and move on. Protecting your remaining time is more valuable than one question.
  • Glance at the answer options before calculating. Spread options = estimate. Clustered options = calculate precisely.
  • Three calculator killers: decimal placement (0.68 not 6.8), unit mismatch (£k vs £m), and stale display (use STO/RCL memory functions). Store Revenue → A, Costs → B, Profit → C.
  • Sanity check every answer in 5 seconds: right magnitude, right direction, reasonable in context. This catches errors that otherwise cost you 60+ seconds.
