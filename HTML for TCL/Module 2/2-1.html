Module 2 · Lesson 2.1

Percentages Mastery

The most frequently tested topic in UK finance aptitude tests. Every SHL, Aon, and Korn Ferry numerical test includes percentage questions — usually multiple.

1

Why Percentages Dominate Finance Tests

Percentages appear in virtually every numerical reasoning test used by UK finance employers. They're not just "a topic" — they're the foundation that most other questions are built on. Data interpretation questions about revenue growth, profit margins, market share, and cost analysis all require percentage fluency.

The issue isn't that percentages are hard. Most candidates know what 15% means. The issue is speed and trap avoidance under pressure. SHL gives you roughly 60–90 seconds per question. In that window, you need to identify which type of percentage calculation is being asked, execute it cleanly, and avoid the deliberate wrong answers designed to catch common errors.

This lesson covers four percentage skills in order of increasing difficulty. Each one builds on the last.

2

Finding a Percentage of an Amount

This is the most basic percentage operation, but the method you choose determines your speed. There are three approaches — use whichever is fastest for the numbers in front of you.

Core Method
X% of Y = Y × (X ÷ 100)
Convert the percentage to a decimal, then multiply.
Worked Example 1 What is 35% of £840,000?
1Convert: 35% = 0.35
2Multiply: £840,000 × 0.35 = £294,000
Worked Example 2 What is 17.5% of £6,400?
1Convert: 17.5% = 0.175
2Multiply: £6,400 × 0.175 = £1,120
3

Percentage Increase & Decrease

When a value increases by a percentage, most candidates calculate the percentage amount and then add it. This works, but it's slower and more error-prone than the multiplier method.

The Multiplier Method
Increase of X% → multiply by (1 + X/100)
Decrease of X% → multiply by (1 − X/100)
One step instead of two. Faster and less error-prone.

Worked Example 3 A department's budget of £240,000 is increased by 12%. What is the new budget?
1Multiplier for +12% = 1.12
2£240,000 × 1.12 = £268,800
Slow Method
12% of £240,000 = £28,800
£240,000 + £28,800 = £268,800
£268,800
Correct answer, but two separate calculations. More chances to make an arithmetic error under pressure.
Fast Method
£240,000 × 1.12
£268,800
Same answer, one calculation. This is the method you should default to in every timed test.
Worked Example 4 A company's workforce of 1,800 is reduced by 15%. How many employees remain?
1Multiplier for −15% = 0.85
21,800 × 0.85 = 1,530 employees
4

Calculating Percentage Change

This is the inverse problem: you're given the old value and the new value, and you need to find the percentage change. This appears in almost every data interpretation question about growth, decline, or comparison.

Percentage Change Formula
% Change = (New − Old) ÷ Old × 100
Always divide by the OLD (original) value. This is where the trap lives.
Worked Example 5 Revenue rose from £550,000 to £680,000. What is the percentage increase?
1Difference = £680,000 − £550,000 = £130,000
2Divide by OLD: £130,000 ÷ £550,000 = 0.2364
3Convert: 0.2364 × 100 = 23.6%
The #1 Percentage Trap

Dividing by the new value instead of the old value is the single most common percentage error in aptitude tests. In the example above, dividing £130,000 by £680,000 gives 19.1% — and that will be one of the answer options. Test designers include it deliberately. Always divide by the starting figure.

Wrong — Dividing by New
£130,000 ÷ £680,000 × 100
19.1% ✗
This is the trap answer. It divides by the new value, not the original.
Right — Dividing by Old
£130,000 ÷ £550,000 × 100
23.6%
Correct. Always divide by the starting (old) value.
5

Reverse Percentages

A reverse percentage question gives you the result of a percentage change and asks you to find the original value. These are the questions that eliminate the most candidates in timed tests, because the obvious approach is wrong.

Reverse Percentage Formula
If value after X% increase = V, then original = V ÷ (1 + X/100)
If value after X% decrease = V, then original = V ÷ (1 − X/100)
Divide by the multiplier, don't subtract/add the percentage.
Worked Example 6 A department's budget was reduced by 12% to £352,000. What was the original budget?
1After a 12% decrease, the multiplier is 0.88
2£352,000 = original × 0.88
3Original = £352,000 ÷ 0.88 = £400,000
Wrong — Adding 12% Back
12% of £352,000 = £42,240
£352,000 + £42,240
£394,240 ✗
This adds 12% of the reduced amount, not 12% of the original. The base has changed — you can't just reverse the arithmetic.
Right — Dividing by Multiplier
£352,000 ÷ 0.88
£400,000
Correct. Dividing by the decrease multiplier recovers the original value.
Why This Trap Works

12% of £400,000 = £48,000. But 12% of £352,000 = £42,240. They're different amounts because 12% of a smaller number is a smaller number. Adding 12% of the reduced value back does not undo a 12% reduction. You must divide by the multiplier.

Worked Example 7 After a 20% price increase, a product costs £54. What was the original price?
1Multiplier for +20% = 1.20
2Original = £54 ÷ 1.20 = £45

Check: £45 × 1.20 = £54 ✓

Worked Example 8 — Finance Context A product's price including 20% VAT is £186. What is the price before VAT?

VAT is a percentage increase on the pre-VAT price. This is a reverse percentage.

1Price including 20% VAT → multiplier = 1.20
2Pre-VAT price = £186 ÷ 1.20 = £155

Common wrong answer: £186 − 20% of £186 = £186 − £37.20 = £148.80 ✗

How to Spot Reverse Percentage Questions

Look for language like: "after an increase of X%", "following a reduction of X%", "including X% tax", "a price that has been marked up by X%". Whenever the question gives you the result and asks for the original, you need to divide by the multiplier — not add or subtract the percentage.

6

Successive Percentage Changes

When a value changes by multiple percentages in sequence — for example, rising 15% one year then falling 8% the next — you cannot simply add or subtract the percentages. Each change applies to a different base.

Successive Changes Formula
Final = Original × Multiplier₁ × Multiplier₂ × …
Chain the multipliers. Never add or subtract percentages.
Worked Example 9 Revenue was £2.4m in Q1. It rose 15% in Q2, then fell 8% in Q3. What was Q3 revenue?
1Q2 = £2.4m × 1.15 = £2.76m
2Q3 = £2.76m × 0.92 = £2,539,200

Or in one step: £2.4m × 1.15 × 0.92 = £2.4m × 1.058 = £2,539,200

Wrong — Adding Percentages
+15% then −8% = +7%?
£2.4m × 1.07 = £2,568,000
£2,568,000 ✗
Adding percentages ignores that the 8% decrease applies to the higher Q2 value, not the original Q1 value.
Right — Chaining Multipliers
£2.4m × 1.15 × 0.92
£2,539,200
Each multiplier applies to the result of the previous one, not the original.
Worked Example 10 Share prices rose 20% on Monday, fell 10% on Tuesday, and rose 5% on Wednesday. What is the net change?
1Combined multiplier = 1.20 × 0.90 × 1.05
2= 1.08 × 1.05 = 1.134
3Net change = 1.134 − 1 = 0.134 = +13.4%

The trap answer (adding: 20 − 10 + 5 = 15%) will be on the answer sheet.

Quick Check

If you see a question with multiple percentage changes in sequence, the answer is almost never the result of simple addition/subtraction of the percentages. If your calculation gives the "obvious" answer, you've probably fallen into the trap.

7

Finance Applications

In real aptitude tests, percentages rarely appear as naked calculations. They're wrapped in finance contexts — profit margins, tax calculations, discount pricing, year-on-year growth. The maths is the same, but you need to know which formula to apply.

Worked Example 11 — Profit Margin A product costs £80 to produce and sells for £140. Overheads are 15% of revenue. What is the profit margin?
1Revenue = £140
2Overhead = 15% of £140 = £21
3Total cost = £80 + £21 = £101
4Profit = £140 − £101 = £39
5Profit margin = £39 ÷ £140 × 100 = 27.9%

Common trap: calculating margin on cost (£39 ÷ £101 = 38.6%) instead of revenue. Profit margin is always profit ÷ revenue.

Worked Example 12 — Compound Interest An investment of £15,000 grows at 4.5% compound interest annually. What is the value after 3 years?
1Multiplier = 1.045
2After 3 years: £15,000 × 1.045 × 1.045 × 1.045
3= £15,000 × 1.045³ = £15,000 × 1.14117 = £17,117

Simple interest trap: £15,000 × 4.5% × 3 = £2,025 → £17,025. Compound gives £17,117 — the difference is small but it's always an answer option.

Worked Example 13 — Stacked Discounts A supplier offers 20% trade discount plus an additional 5% for early payment. What is the final price on a £4,000 order?

This is successive percentage changes — multiply, don't add.

1After 20% discount: £4,000 × 0.80 = £3,200
2After additional 5%: £3,200 × 0.95 = £3,040

Trap answer: 25% off = £3,000. The actual discount is 24% (1 − 0.80 × 0.95 = 0.76 = 24% off), not 25%.

Practice Questions

Work through each question before revealing the answer. These cover all four percentage skills from this lesson.

Practice Question 1
A company's annual revenue is £2.8 million. The marketing budget is 8.5% of revenue. How much is the marketing budget?
Show Answer ▾
1£2,800,000 × 0.085 = £238,000
Practice Question 2
Operating costs fell from £1.25 million to £1.08 million. What was the percentage decrease?
Show Answer ▾
1Difference = £1.25m − £1.08m = £0.17m
2% change = £0.17m ÷ £1.25m × 100 = 13.6% decrease

Divide by the old value (£1.25m), not the new value. £0.17m ÷ £1.08m = 15.7% is the trap.

Practice Question 3
After a 15% salary increase, an employee earns £46,000. What was their previous salary?
Show Answer ▾
1Multiplier for +15% = 1.15
2Original = £46,000 ÷ 1.15 = £40,000

Trap: £46,000 − 15% of £46,000 = £46,000 − £6,900 = £39,100 ✗

Practice Question 4
A portfolio gained 12% in Year 1, lost 5% in Year 2, and gained 8% in Year 3. Starting value: £50,000. What is the final value?
Show Answer ▾
1Combined multiplier = 1.12 × 0.95 × 1.08
2= 1.064 × 1.08 = 1.14912
3£50,000 × 1.14912 = £57,456

Trap: adding percentages (12 − 5 + 8 = 15%) gives £57,500. Close — but wrong.

Practice Question 5
A consultancy charges £180 per hour. They apply a 10% volume discount for contracts over 100 hours, plus a further 5% discount for annual retainer clients. What is the effective hourly rate for a retainer client on a 120-hour contract?
Show Answer ▾
1After 10% volume discount: £180 × 0.90 = £162
2After 5% retainer discount: £162 × 0.95 = £153.90

Combined discount = 1 − (0.90 × 0.95) = 1 − 0.855 = 14.5%, not 15%. Trap: £180 × 0.85 = £153.00 ✗

Practice Question 6
A product's price including 20% VAT is £276. The company then applies a 25% trade discount to the pre-VAT price. What does the customer pay (excluding VAT)?
Show Answer ▾

Two steps: reverse percentage to find pre-VAT, then apply discount.

1Pre-VAT price = £276 ÷ 1.20 = £230
2After 25% trade discount: £230 × 0.75 = £172.50

This combines reverse percentages with successive changes — a common pattern in harder questions.

Key Takeaways

  • Use the multiplier method for increases and decreases — one step, not two. Memorise common multipliers (×1.08, ×0.85, etc.).
  • Percentage change: always divide by the OLD value. Dividing by the new value is the most common trap in SHL tests.
  • Reverse percentages: divide by the multiplier, don't add/subtract the percentage from the result. Look for "after", "including", and "following" as trigger words.
  • Successive changes: chain multipliers. Never add or subtract percentages applied to different base values.
  • Build awkward percentages from easy pieces: 10%, 5%, 1%, 25%, 50% are your building blocks.
  • Profit margin is always calculated on revenue, not on cost. This appears in almost every finance-context percentage question.
