Ratios & Proportions

The second most tested numerical skill in UK finance aptitude tests. Ratios appear in staffing questions, financial analysis, resource allocation, and every data interpretation section.

1

Why Ratios Matter in Finance Tests

Ratios test your ability to compare quantities, allocate resources, and scale values — all fundamental skills in finance. In real SHL and Aon tests, ratios rarely appear as straightforward "simplify 12:8" questions. Instead, they're embedded in scenarios: dividing a budget between departments, calculating staffing requirements, or interpreting financial ratios from a data table.

The concepts are simple. The difficulty comes from working quickly with unfamiliar numbers, and from the specific traps that test designers build into the answer options.

2

Simplifying Ratios

A ratio compares two or more quantities. Simplifying means dividing all parts by their highest common factor (HCF) to get the smallest whole numbers.

Method
Divide every part of the ratio by the HCF
If you can't spot the HCF, divide by any common factor and repeat until simplified.
Worked Example 1 Simplify 150 : 250 : 100
1All divisible by 50: 150÷50 = 3, 250÷50 = 5, 100÷50 = 2
2Simplified: 3 : 5 : 2
Worked Example 2 Express the ratio £1.20 : £3.00 in simplest form
1Convert to same units: 120p : 300p
2HCF of 120 and 300 = 60
3120÷60 : 300÷60 = 2 : 5
Common Trap

When the ratio involves different units (e.g. £ and pence, metres and cm, hours and minutes), you must convert to the same unit first. Test designers include answers based on the unconverted numbers.

3

Dividing a Quantity in a Given Ratio

This is the most common ratio question type in finance aptitude tests: splitting a total amount between two or more parts according to a ratio. The method is always the same.

3-Step Method
① Add the ratio parts   ② Divide total by sum   ③ Multiply each part
Total parts → value of one part → value of each share.
Worked Example 3 A £360,000 budget is split between departments A, B, and C in the ratio 3 : 2 : 1. How much does each get?
1Total parts = 3 + 2 + 1 = 6
2Value of 1 part = £360,000 ÷ 6 = £60,000
3A = 3 × £60,000 = £180,000
4B = 2 × £60,000 = £120,000
5C = 1 × £60,000 = £60,000

Check: £180,000 + £120,000 + £60,000 = £360,000 ✓

Worked Example 4 A profit of £84,000 is shared between two partners in the ratio 5 : 7. How much does the smaller share receive?
1Total parts = 5 + 7 = 12
2Value of 1 part = £84,000 ÷ 12 = £7,000
3Smaller share = 5 × £7,000 = £35,000
The Ratio Trap

A common wrong answer is to divide the total by one of the ratio numbers directly. In Example 3, dividing £360,000 by 3 gives £120,000 — which is Department B's share, not Department A's. The test will include this as an option. Always find the total number of parts first.

Wrong — Dividing by Ratio Number
£360,000 ÷ 3 = £120,000
£120,000 ✗
This divides by one part of the ratio, ignoring the other parts.
Right — Dividing by Total Parts
£360,000 ÷ 6 = £60,000 per part
3 parts = 3 × £60,000
£180,000 ✓
4

Finding Unknown Quantities from a Ratio

Sometimes you're given one part of the ratio and asked to find the total or another part. The method: find the value of one "unit" first, then scale.

Worked Example 5 Senior and junior staff are in the ratio 3 : 5. If there are 90 senior staff, how many junior staff are there?
13 parts = 90 staff → 1 part = 90 ÷ 3 = 30
2Junior = 5 parts = 5 × 30 = 150 junior staff
Worked Example 6 A firm's debt-to-equity ratio is 2 : 3. If equity is £450,000, what is the total capital (debt + equity)?
1Equity = 3 parts = £450,000 → 1 part = £150,000
2Debt = 2 parts = 2 × £150,000 = £300,000
3Total capital = £300,000 + £450,000 = £750,000

Or: total parts = 5, so total = 5 × £150,000 = £750,000.

5

Scaling & Changing Ratios

Harder ratio questions involve a ratio that changes — for example, a company adds staff and you need to calculate the new ratio, or you need to determine how much of something to add to change a ratio to a target.

Worked Example 7 A solution mixes water and concentrate in the ratio 4 : 1. If you have 600ml of solution, how much concentrate must you add to change the ratio to 3 : 1?

First, find current amounts:

1Current: 4 + 1 = 5 parts. Water = 600 × 4/5 = 480ml. Concentrate = 120ml.

Water stays at 480ml. New ratio is 3 : 1, so concentrate should be 480 ÷ 3:

2New concentrate needed = 480 ÷ 3 = 160ml
3Extra concentrate = 160 − 120 = 40ml
Worked Example 8 A team of 24 has a manager-to-staff ratio of 1 : 5. After hiring, the team grows to 35. The manager-to-staff ratio is now 1 : 4. How many managers were hired?

Before:

11 + 5 = 6 parts. Managers = 24 ÷ 6 = 4. Staff = 20.

After:

21 + 4 = 5 parts. Managers = 35 ÷ 5 = 7. Staff = 28.
3Managers hired = 7 − 4 = 3 managers

Staff hired = 28 − 20 = 8. Total hired = 3 + 8 = 11. Check: 24 + 11 = 35 ✓

6

Direct & Inverse Proportion

Direct proportion: when one quantity doubles, the other doubles. Cost and quantity, revenue and units sold. The ratio between them stays constant.

Inverse proportion: when one quantity doubles, the other halves. Workers and time, speed and journey time. The product stays constant.

Direct Proportion
If A doubles, B doubles → A/B = constant
Inverse Proportion
If A doubles, B halves → A × B = constant
Worked Example 9 — Direct If 12 units cost £84, how much do 20 units cost?
1Cost per unit = £84 ÷ 12 = £7
220 units = 20 × £7 = £140
Worked Example 10 — Inverse 8 workers complete a project in 15 days. How long would 12 workers take?
1Total work = 8 × 15 = 120 worker-days
212 workers: 120 ÷ 12 = 10 days
Wrong — Treating as Direct
More workers = more time?
15 × (12/8) = 22.5 days
22.5 days ✗
This treats it as direct proportion — more workers, more days. The opposite is true.
Right — Inverse Proportion
Total work = 8 × 15 = 120
120 ÷ 12
10 days ✓
More workers = less time. The product (workers × days) stays constant.
How to Tell the Difference

Ask yourself: if the first quantity increases, does the second increase (direct) or decrease (inverse)? More workers → less time = inverse. More items → higher cost = direct. If in doubt, calculate the "total work" or "unit rate" as an intermediate step.

7

Finance Applications

In real aptitude tests, ratio questions are dressed in finance language. You need to recognise the ratio structure underneath. Here are the most common finance ratio formats.

Worked Example 11 — Price-to-Earnings Company X has a P/E ratio of 18. Earnings per share are £2.40. What is the share price?

P/E ratio = Price ÷ Earnings. So Price = P/E × Earnings.

1Share price = 18 × £2.40 = £43.20
Worked Example 12 — Staffing Ratio A consultancy maintains a partner-to-associate ratio of 1 : 6. The firm has 14 partners. It plans to hire enough associates to change the ratio to 1 : 8. How many associates must be hired?
1Current associates = 14 × 6 = 84
2Target associates = 14 × 8 = 112
3Associates to hire = 112 − 84 = 28
Worked Example 13 — Revenue Split Three regional offices generate revenue in the ratio 5 : 3 : 2. The East office (smallest share) generated £1.8m. What was the total revenue?
1East = 2 parts = £1.8m → 1 part = £0.9m
2Total = 5 + 3 + 2 = 10 parts = 10 × £0.9m = £9.0m
Watch For

Questions that give you one share and ask for the total — or give you the total and ask for the difference between two shares. Always establish the value of one part first. This works regardless of how many parts or how complex the question.

19 parts. Analysts = 45 × 7/9 = 35. Managers = 10.

Key Takeaways

  • To divide a quantity in a ratio: add the parts, divide the total by the sum, then multiply each part. Find the value of one part first — this solves everything.
  • When given one share of a ratio and asked for the total (or another share), work out what one part equals and scale from there.
  • Convert to the same units before simplifying a ratio. Mixed units are a deliberate trap.
  • Direct proportion: both quantities change in the same direction. Inverse proportion: one goes up, the other goes down. Use "total work" as your intermediate step for inverse problems.
  • Finance ratios (P/E, debt-to-equity, staffing) are just regular ratios in context. Identify which value maps to which part of the ratio.
  • The most common trap: dividing the total by one ratio number instead of the sum of all parts.
