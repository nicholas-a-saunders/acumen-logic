Fractions, Decimals & Conversions

Master instant conversions between fractions, decimals, and percentages — plus currency and unit conversions that appear in every finance test.

1

The Conversion Triangle

Every aptitude test question involving fractions, decimals, or percentages requires you to move between these three formats. The method is always the same — the speed at which you execute it determines your score.

Fraction → Decimal: divide numerator by denominator. Decimal → Percentage: multiply by 100. Percentage → Decimal: divide by 100. Decimal → Fraction: write as a fraction over a power of 10, then simplify.

Core Conversion Chain
³⁄₈ → 3 ÷ 8 = 0.375 → × 100 = 37.5%
Fraction → decimal → percentage. Reverse the steps to go backwards.
Worked Example 1 Convert ³⁄₈ to a decimal and a percentage.
1Fraction → Decimal: 3 ÷ 8 = 0.375
2Decimal → Percentage: 0.375 × 100 = 37.5%
Worked Example 2 Convert 62.5% to a fraction in its simplest form.
1Percentage → Decimal: 62.5 ÷ 100 = 0.625
2Decimal → Fraction: 0.625 = 625/1000
3Simplify: 625 ÷ 125 = 5, 1000 ÷ 125 = 8 → ⁵⁄₈

Common Trap — Thirds Are Recurring

⅓ of £840,000 = £280,000 exactly. But ⅓ of £850,000 = £283,333.33. When a test says "to the nearest whole number," you must round — not truncate. The wrong answer option will always be the truncated value.

3

Comparing Values Across Formats

Test questions frequently present values in mixed formats and ask you to identify the largest or smallest. The method is always the same: convert everything to decimals, then compare.

The Rule
Mixed formats? Convert all to decimals first.
Never estimate mixed-format comparisons by eye. Always convert.
Worked Example 3 Which is the largest: ⁷⁄₂₀, 0.38, or 34%?
1Convert ⁷⁄₂₀: 7 ÷ 20 = 0.35
20.38 is already a decimal
3Convert 34%: 34 ÷ 100 = 0.34
4Compare: 0.34 < 0.35 < 0.38 → 0.38 is largest
Worked Example 4 — Finance Context A fund's quarterly returns were: Q1 = ³⁄₁₆, Q2 = 17.5%, Q3 = 0.19, Q4 = ⁹⁄₅₀. In which quarter was the return highest?
1Q1: 3 ÷ 16 = 0.1875
2Q2: 17.5 ÷ 100 = 0.175
3Q3: 0.19
4Q4: 9 ÷ 50 = 0.18
5Order: 0.175 < 0.18 < 0.1875 < 0.19 → Q3 (0.19)
Watch Out

⁷⁄₂₀ looks larger than 34% at first glance, but it isn't. Fractions feel "bigger" than they are because the numbers are whole. Never estimate — always convert and compare on the same scale.

4

Adding & Subtracting Fractions

Finance tests love "remainder" questions: a budget or portfolio is split into named fractions, and you must find what's left. The method requires a lowest common denominator (LCD).

The Method
Find LCD → Convert each fraction → Add numerators → Subtract from 1
Same denominator? Add/subtract numerators directly. Different? Find the LCD first.
Worked Example 5 A portfolio is ²⁄₅ equities and ¼ bonds. What fraction is allocated to other assets?
1LCD of 5 and 4 = 20
2Convert: ²⁄₅ = ⁸⁄₂₀, ¼ = ⁵⁄₂₀
3Sum: ⁸⁄₂₀ + ⁵⁄₂₀ = ¹³⁄₂₀
4Remainder: ²⁰⁄₂₀ − ¹³⁄₂₀ = ⁷⁄₂₀ (35%)
Worked Example 6 A company's annual budget is split: ⅓ on salaries, ¼ on premises, ⅙ on marketing. The remaining budget is £450,000. What is the total budget?
1LCD of 3, 4, and 6 = 12
2Convert: ⅓ = ⁴⁄₁₂, ¼ = ³⁄₁₂, ⅙ = ²⁄₁₂
3Sum: ⁴⁄₁₂ + ³⁄₁₂ + ²⁄₁₂ = ⁹⁄₁₂ = ¾
4Remainder: 1 − ¾ = ¼ of total budget
5¼ of total = £450,000 → total = £450,000 × 4 = £1,800,000
The #1 Fraction Trap

Adding denominators instead of finding the LCD. Students write ⅓ + ¼ + ⅙ = ³⁄₁₃ by adding the bottom numbers. This produces a remainder of ¹⁰⁄₁₃ — which gives an answer not in the options. If your answer doesn't match any option, you've almost certainly added denominators instead of converting to a common one.

5

Multiplying Fractions

Fraction multiplication appears when you need "a fraction of a fraction" — for example, "¾ of employees are full-time, and ⅔ of full-time employees are based in London." The key technique is cross-cancelling before multiplying to keep numbers small.

Key Rule
Simplify BEFORE multiplying
Cross-cancel common factors between any numerator and any denominator. Smaller numbers = faster mental maths.
Worked Example 7 A firm has 480 employees. ¾ work full-time. ⅔ of full-time staff are based in London. How many full-time London staff?
1Set up: ¾ × ⅔ × 480
2Cross-cancel: the 3 in ¾ cancels with the 3 in ⅔ → ¹⁄₄ × ² = ²⁄₄ = ½
3Apply: ½ × 480 = 240
Slow Method
¾ × ⅔ × 480
= (3 × 2 × 480) ÷ (4 × 3)
= 2,880 ÷ 12
240
Correct, but large numbers and harder mental maths. More time on calculator.
Fast Method
Cancel the 3s first:
¾ × ⅔ = ½
½ × 480
240
Small numbers, instant mental maths. ~8 seconds saved per question.
Worked Example 8 — Finance Context A £1.2 million marketing budget: ⅗ is digital, and ⅚ of the digital budget goes to paid search. What is the paid search budget?
1Set up: ⅗ × ⅚ × £1,200,000
2Cross-cancel: the 5 in ⅗ cancels with the 5 in ⅚ → ³⁄₁ × ¹⁄₆ = ³⁄₆ = ½
3Apply: ½ × £1,200,000 = £600,000
6

Recurring Decimals & Rounding

Some fractions produce recurring decimals. Tests handle this in two ways: they give you exact fractions in the answer options, or they ask you to round to a specified number of decimal places. The critical rule is: only round at the very last step.

FractionExact DecimalRounded (3 d.p.)
⅓0.3333…0.333
⅔0.6666…0.667
⅙0.1666…0.167
⅐0.142857…0.143
⅑0.1111…0.111
Precision Tip

When your answer is a decimal and the question says "to 2 decimal places," only round at the very last step. Rounding intermediate calculations compounds rounding errors and can push you to the wrong answer choice. Keep full precision on your calculator until the final answer.

7

Currency & Unit Conversions

Currency and unit conversions appear frequently in finance aptitude tests. The core skill is directional logic: knowing when to multiply and when to divide. Get the direction wrong and you'll find your answer among the trap options.

The Direction Rule
£1 = $1.25 → multiply £ by 1.25 to get $ · divide $ by 1.25 to get £
Going TO the larger-number currency? Multiply. Going TO the smaller-number currency? Divide.
Worked Example 9 — Simple Conversion A London office spends £48,000 on software licences priced in US dollars. The exchange rate is £1 = $1.26. What is the cost in USD?
1Direction: converting FROM £ TO $ → multiply
2£48,000 × 1.26 = $60,480
Worked Example 10 — Cross-Currency Chain A firm receives €72,000. The rates are: £1 = €1.16 and £1 = $1.26. What is the equivalent in USD?
1Convert € to £: €72,000 ÷ 1.16 = £62,068.97
2Convert £ to $: £62,068.97 × 1.26 = $78,206.90

Always chain through GBP when no direct cross-rate is given.

Common Trap
€72,000 × 1.16 = €83,520
Wrong direction
Multiplying by 1.16 converts £ to € — not € to £. The result is larger, which makes no sense (€ are worth less than £).
Correct Method
€72,000 ÷ 1.16 = £62,069
Divide to get £
Since £1 = €1.16, you divide by 1.16 to go from € back to £. The GBP amount should be smaller.
Sanity Check

After converting, ask: "Does the answer make sense?" If £1 buys more than 1 of the foreign currency, then the foreign-currency amount should always be larger than the GBP amount. If your answer goes the wrong way, you've multiplied when you should have divided (or vice versa).

8

Exam-Style Question

SHL-Style Question A company has a £1.2 million annual marketing budget allocated as follows: Digital Advertising = ⅓, Events & Conferences = ¼, Print & PR = ⅙, Market Research = remainder. How much is allocated to Market Research?

Options: (A) £200,000   (B) £250,000   (C) £300,000   (D) £350,000

1LCD of 3, 4, and 6 = 12
2Convert: ⅓ = ⁴⁄₁₂, ¼ = ³⁄₁₂, ⅙ = ²⁄₁₂
3Sum: ⁴⁄₁₂ + ³⁄₁₂ + ²⁄₁₂ = ⁹⁄₁₂ = ¾
4Remainder: 1 − ¾ = ¼
5Calculate: ¼ × £1,200,000 = £300,000 → Answer: C

Common mistake: adding the denominators (⅓ + ¼ + ⅙ = ³⁄₁₃) instead of finding the LCD. This gives a remainder of ¹⁰⁄₁₃ — which produces an answer not matching any option, a signal you've gone wrong.

Key Takeaways

  • Convert everything to decimals when comparing mixed-format values — never estimate by eye.
  • Memorise the essential conversion table: halves, thirds, quarters, fifths, and eighths. These appear in almost every test.
  • For "remainder" questions: find the LCD, sum the named fractions, subtract from 1, then multiply by the total.
  • When multiplying fractions, always cross-cancel before multiplying — it halves your calculation time and keeps numbers manageable.
  • Only round at the very last step. Rounding intermediate calculations can push you to the wrong answer option.
  • Currency conversions are about direction: multiply to go TO the bigger-number currency, divide to go back. Always sanity-check the result.
