Averages, Growth & Compound Change

Weighted averages, CAGR, and sequential percentage changes — the calculations that separate top scores from average ones.

1

Simple vs Weighted Averages

A simple average (mean) treats every value equally: add them up and divide by the count. A weighted average gives each value a different importance based on its weight — the number of items, revenue, or population it represents.

Finance tests almost always require weighted averages. The simple average is nearly always the trap answer.

Weighted Average Formula
Weighted Average = Sum of (Value × Weight) ÷ Sum of Weights
Each value is multiplied by its weight before averaging.
Worked Example 1 A company has 3 offices. London: 200 staff, average salary £52,000. Manchester: 80 staff, average salary £38,000. Edinburgh: 40 staff, average salary £41,000. What is the company-wide average salary?
1London total: 200 × £52,000 = £10,400,000
2Manchester total: 80 × £38,000 = £3,040,000
3Edinburgh total: 40 × £41,000 = £1,640,000
4Grand total: £10,400,000 + £3,040,000 + £1,640,000 = £15,080,000
5Total staff: 200 + 80 + 40 = 320
6Weighted average: £15,080,000 ÷ 320 = £47,125
Simple Average (Trap)
(£52,000 + £38,000 + £41,000) ÷ 3
£43,667
Treats each office equally — ignoring that London has 200 staff vs Edinburgh's 40. This will always be a wrong option.
Weighted Average (Correct)
Total salary bill ÷ total staff
£47,125
London's 200 staff pull the average up significantly. The weighted average is always closer to the largest group's value.
2

The "Average of Averages" Fallacy

This is the single most common numerical reasoning trap. When you're given averages for different groups, you cannot simply average those averages — unless every group has the exact same size. In finance tests, the groups are almost never equal.

Critical Rule

If a question gives you averages for groups of different sizes and asks for the overall average, you must use weighted averaging. Averaging the averages will always give you a wrong answer — and it will always be one of the multiple-choice options.

Worked Example 2 A fund invests in two sectors. Technology: 60% of the fund, returned 14%. Healthcare: 40% of the fund, returned 8%. What was the fund's overall return?
1Technology contribution: 60% × 14% = 8.4%
2Healthcare contribution: 40% × 8% = 3.2%
3Overall return: 8.4% + 3.2% = 11.6%

Trap answer: (14% + 8%) ÷ 2 = 11%. This ignores that Tech is a larger allocation.

3

Sequential Percentage Changes

When a value changes by a percentage and then changes by another percentage, you cannot simply add the percentages. Each change applies to a different base. This is the foundation of compound growth — and a major trap source.

Sequential Change Method
Multiply by successive multipliers: × 1.10 × 0.95 × 1.08 ...
+10% → × 1.10 · −5% → × 0.95 · +8% → × 1.08. Chain them together.
Worked Example 3 A share price starts at £200. It rises 15% in Year 1, falls 10% in Year 2, then rises 20% in Year 3. What is the price after Year 3?
1Year 1: £200 × 1.15 = £230
2Year 2: £230 × 0.90 = £207
3Year 3: £207 × 1.20 = £248.40

Trap: +15% − 10% + 20% = +25%, so £200 × 1.25 = £250. Wrong — because each % applies to a different base.

The "Up Then Down" Trap

A value rises by 20% then falls by 20%. Most candidates think it returns to the original. It doesn't. £100 × 1.20 = £120 × 0.80 = £96. A 20% rise followed by a 20% fall always results in a net loss of 4%. The bigger the percentage, the bigger the asymmetry.

Worked Example 4 A company's revenue was £4.8 million. It grew 12% in Q1, fell 5% in Q2, grew 8% in Q3, and fell 3% in Q4. What was revenue at year-end?
1Combined multiplier: 1.12 × 0.95 × 1.08 × 0.97
2Step by step: 1.12 × 0.95 = 1.064
31.064 × 1.08 = 1.14912
41.14912 × 0.97 = 1.114646
5£4,800,000 × 1.114646 = £5,350,303

Net change: +11.46%, not +12% (the sum of 12 − 5 + 8 − 3 = 12%). The difference matters at this scale.

4

CAGR — Compound Annual Growth Rate

CAGR tells you the single steady growth rate that would take a starting value to an ending value over a given time period. It smooths out the volatility of sequential changes into one clean number — essential for comparing investments or revenue growth.

CAGR Formula
CAGR = (End Value ÷ Start Value)^(1/n) − 1
Where n = number of years. The result is a decimal — multiply by 100 for a percentage.
Worked Example 5 A portfolio was worth £120,000 three years ago and is now worth £155,520. What is the CAGR?
1Ratio: £155,520 ÷ £120,000 = 1.296
2Apply formula: 1.296^(1/3) = 1.296^0.3333
3Calculate: 1.296^0.3333 = 1.09
4CAGR = 1.09 − 1 = 0.09 = 9% per year

Verification: £120,000 × 1.09 × 1.09 × 1.09 = £120,000 × 1.295029 ≈ £155,403 (rounding accounts for the small difference).

Test Tip — CAGR Without a Calculator

Many tests don't expect you to calculate nth roots by hand. Instead, they give you the answer options and expect you to work backwards: try each growth rate and see which one produces the correct end value. If the options are 7%, 8%, 9%, 10% — try 9% first (middle option) and adjust.

Worked Example 6 — Working Backwards Revenue grew from £2 million to £2.662 million over 3 years. Options: (A) 8% (B) 10% (C) 12% (D) 15%. What is the CAGR?
1Try option B (10%): £2m × 1.10³ = £2m × 1.331 = £2,662,000
2That matches exactly → Answer: B (10%)

In a real test, start with the middle options. If the result is too high, try a lower rate. If too low, try higher. You'll rarely need more than 2 attempts.

5

Compound Growth Applications

Compound growth appears in many finance contexts: interest calculations, population growth, depreciation, and inflation. The key insight is that compound growth is exponential, not linear — the difference between simple and compound growth widens dramatically over time.

Compound Growth Formula
Final Value = Start Value × (1 + rate)^n
For depreciation, use (1 − rate)^n instead.
Worked Example 7 — Depreciation A company buys IT equipment for £180,000. It depreciates at 25% per year. What is the book value after 3 years?
1Multiplier for 25% depreciation: 1 − 0.25 = 0.75
2After 3 years: £180,000 × 0.75³ = £180,000 × 0.421875
3= £75,937.50

Trap answer: £180,000 − (3 × 25% × £180,000) = £180,000 − £135,000 = £45,000. This uses simple depreciation (straight-line), not compound. The compound value is always higher because each year's depreciation applies to a smaller base.

Worked Example 8 — Inflation Inflation averages 3.5% per year. A basket of goods costs £850 today. What will it cost in 4 years, to the nearest pound?
1Multiplier: 1.035
2After 4 years: £850 × 1.035⁴ = £850 × 1.14752
3= £975.39 → £975

Simple inflation trap: £850 + (4 × 3.5% × £850) = £850 + £119 = £969. The compound answer is £6 higher — enough to pick the wrong option.

Practice Questions

Work through each question before revealing the answer. Target: 90 seconds per question.

Practice Question 1
A retailer has two stores. Store A: 150 transactions per day, average value £34. Store B: 250 transactions per day, average value £28. What is the company-wide average transaction value?
Show Answer ▾
1Store A total: 150 × £34 = £5,100
2Store B total: 250 × £28 = £7,000
3Combined: £12,100 ÷ 400 = £30.25

Trap: (£34 + £28) ÷ 2 = £31. The weighted average is pulled toward Store B because it has more transactions.

Practice Question 2
A property is valued at £320,000. It rises 8% in Year 1, rises 5% in Year 2, and falls 12% in Year 3. What is the value after Year 3, to the nearest thousand?
Show Answer ▾
1Multiplier chain: 1.08 × 1.05 × 0.88 = 0.99792
2£320,000 × 0.99792 = £319,334 ≈ £319,000

Trap: +8% + 5% − 12% = +1%, giving £323,200. The actual result is a slight loss, not a gain. The 12% fall on the inflated base wipes out the earlier gains.

Practice Question 3
An investment grew from £50,000 to £66,550 over 3 years. What is the CAGR? Options: (A) 8% (B) 10% (C) 11% (D) 12%
Show Answer ▾
1Try 10%: £50,000 × 1.10³ = £50,000 × 1.331 = £66,550
2Exact match → Answer: B (10%)
Practice Question 4
A vehicle fleet worth £2.4 million depreciates at 18% per year (compound). What is the fleet value after 2 years?
Show Answer ▾
1Multiplier: 1 − 0.18 = 0.82
2After 2 years: £2,400,000 × 0.82² = £2,400,000 × 0.6724
3= £1,613,760

Trap: straight-line depreciation gives £2,400,000 − (2 × 18% × £2,400,000) = £2,400,000 − £864,000 = £1,536,000 — significantly lower than the compound answer.

Practice Question 5
Three departments contribute to a company's customer satisfaction score. Retail (500 customers): 82%. Corporate (200 customers): 91%. Private Banking (50 customers): 96%. What is the company-wide satisfaction score?
Show Answer ▾
1Retail: 500 × 82 = 41,000
2Corporate: 200 × 91 = 18,200
3Private Banking: 50 × 96 = 4,800
4Total: 64,000 ÷ 750 = 85.3%

Trap: (82 + 91 + 96) ÷ 3 = 89.7%. The weighted result is much lower because Retail (the lowest score) has the most customers.

Practice Question 6
A house was worth £280,000 five years ago and is now worth £365,162. What is the approximate annual growth rate? Options: (A) 4.5% (B) 5.0% (C) 5.5% (D) 6.0%
Show Answer ▾
1Try 5.5%: £280,000 × 1.055⁵
21.055⁵ = 1.30696 → £280,000 × 1.30696 = £365,949 — very close
3Try 5.0%: 1.05⁵ = 1.27628 → £280,000 × 1.27628 = £357,359 — too low
45.5% gives the closest match → Answer: C (5.5%)

Key Takeaways

  • Never average averages — always use weighted averaging when groups have different sizes. The simple average is designed to be a trap option.
  • Sequential % changes multiply, they don't add — convert each change to a multiplier and chain them together.
  • A rise of X% then a fall of X% does NOT return to the original — it always produces a net loss. The bigger the %, the bigger the loss.
  • CAGR smooths volatile growth into one rate. In tests, work backwards from the answer options rather than calculating nth roots.
  • Compound vs simple — compound depreciation always leaves a higher value; compound growth always gives a higher result than simple. The difference widens with time.
