What the Diagnostic Measures

The diagnostic is a 20-question timed assessment designed to mirror the format, difficulty, and time pressure of real UK finance aptitude tests. It tests three distinct cognitive categories — the same three that SHL, Aon, Korn Ferry, and Cappfinity assessments evaluate.

The goal is not to "pass" — it's to identify your specific bottleneck so you can train on what actually matters instead of wasting time on areas where you're already strong.

The Three Categories

Questions are interleaved — you'll alternate between categories throughout the test, just as some real assessments do. This prevents you from settling into one mode of thinking and tests your ability to switch between different cognitive skills under pressure.

Numerical Reasoning 7 Questions

Tests your ability to perform calculations accurately and efficiently under pressure. These questions require you to apply mathematical concepts — not just recall formulas, but interpret a scenario and decide which operation to use.

Sequential % changes Compound interest Ratios Profit margins Reverse percentages Multiplicative changes Work-rate problems
Strong threshold: 5+ / 7 correct Course focus: Modules 2 & 3
Data Interpretation 7 Questions

Tests your ability to extract, compare, and draw conclusions from data presented in tables. Each question gives you a data table and asks you to find a specific insight — sometimes requiring multiple calculations across rows and columns.

Table aggregation Revenue per head Consistency & variance Trend projection Total cost comparison Profit maximisation Market share
Strong threshold: 5+ / 7 correct Course focus: Module 3
Logical Reasoning 6 Questions

Tests your ability to draw valid conclusions from given information, recognise patterns, and apply rules accurately. These questions don't involve any calculations — they test the clarity and rigour of your thinking.

Syllogisms Deductive logic Conditional rules Number sequences Logical fallacies Code & cipher logic
Strong threshold: 4+ / 6 correct Course focus: Module 4

What the Questions Look Like

Every question has five answer options. Data Interpretation questions include a data table directly above the question. Here's a flavour of each category:

Numerical Reasoning

"A company's revenue increased by 15% from Q1 to Q2, then decreased by 8% from Q2 to Q3. If Q1 revenue was £2.4 million, what was the Q3 revenue?"

Tests: sequential percentage changes — a classic trap where adding/subtracting percentages gives the wrong answer.

Data Interpretation

"Referring to the table, which region had the highest revenue per employee in Q3?"

Tests: derived metric calculation — you must divide revenue by headcount for each region, not just pick the largest revenue.

Logical Reasoning

"All successful candidates have strong analytical skills. Some candidates with strong analytical skills lack commercial awareness. Therefore…"

Tests: syllogistic reasoning — the conclusion is indeterminate, which most candidates fail to recognise.

This gives you one of eight possible profiles — for example, Strong-Strong-Weak (solid at numbers, but logical reasoning is the bottleneck) or Weak-Strong-Strong (numerical skills need the most work). Your profile tells you exactly where to invest your training time.

Your overall score is also calculated as a simple percentage across all 20 questions, which determines your recommended training track.

Important

A "Weak" classification on the diagnostic does not mean you'll fail real tests. It means that category is where you'll gain the most marks from training. The diagnostic measures your starting point — not your ceiling.

Your Bottleneck

Your primary bottleneck is the category with the lowest score relative to its threshold. This is the single area that, if improved, will have the biggest impact on your overall test performance.

Most candidates assume their bottleneck is numerical — because the maths feels hardest. In practice, Data Interpretation is the most common bottleneck. The reason: DI questions require both mathematical skill and the ability to extract the right numbers from complex tables under pressure. It's the combination that trips people up.

Training Tracks

Based on your overall percentage score, the diagnostic recommends one of three training approaches:

80%+
Advanced Track
Strong foundation already in place. Focus on speed optimisation, complex multi-table scenarios, and provider-specific exam technique. Prioritise Modules 4, 5, and 6.
60–79%
Standard Track
Solid fundamentals with identifiable gaps. Complete all modules in order, with extra attention on your weak categories. This is where most candidates start.
Below 60%
Intensive Track
Extended focus on the fundamentals before advancing. Revisit Module 1's maths refresh, then work through Modules 2 and 3 thoroughly. Speed comes after accuracy is established.
Regardless of Track

Everyone should complete the full course. The tracks determine pacing and emphasis, not which lessons to skip. A candidate scoring 85% still benefits from Module 2 — they just move through it faster.

How to Use Your Results

After completing the diagnostic, you'll see your full breakdown on the results screen — scores by category, your Strong/Weak classification, and your primary bottleneck. You'll also receive this by email. Here's how to act on it:

If Numerical Reasoning is your bottleneck: Modules 2 and 3 are your priority. Spend extra time on the worked examples and repeat the speed drills until the shortcuts become automatic. As your raw numerical fluency improves, your DI score will rise too — because faster calculations mean more time to interpret data.

If Data Interpretation is your bottleneck: Module 3 is critical. The issue is rarely the maths — it's the data extraction. Focus on learning to scan tables systematically: column headers first, then row labels, then the specific cell you need. Practise identifying which data point the question is actually asking for before starting any calculation.

If Logical Reasoning is your bottleneck: Module 4 is your focus. Logical reasoning is the most "learnable" of the three categories — once you recognise the common patterns (syllogisms, conditional rules, sequence logic), your accuracy improves quickly. Most candidates see the biggest percentage point gains here.

Common Mistake

Don't ignore a category just because it's classified as "Strong." A score of 5/7 is technically Strong but still means you got 2 wrong — there's room to improve. The classification tells you where to start, not where to stop.

8

Retaking the Diagnostic

In Module 6, you'll take a full mock assessment that serves as your post-training benchmark. Comparing your Module 6 results against this diagnostic baseline is the clearest way to measure your progress and confirm your training has worked.

Expect to see the biggest improvements in whichever category was classified as "Weak" — that's the whole point of targeted training. Candidates who follow the full course typically move from the 40th–50th percentile range to the 75th–85th percentile on real employer assessments.

Key Takeaways

  • The diagnostic tests three categories: Numerical Reasoning (7 Qs), Data Interpretation (7 Qs), and Logical Reasoning (6 Qs) — interleaved across 15 minutes.
  • Each category is classified as Strong (70%+) or Weak (below 70%), giving you one of eight possible profiles.
  • Your primary bottleneck is the lowest-scoring category — this is where training gives you the highest return on time invested.
  • Data Interpretation is the most common bottleneck, even among candidates who are strong at pure maths.
  • Your overall score determines your training track: Advanced (80%+), Standard (60–79%), or Intensive (below 60%).
  • The diagnostic is a starting point. Module 6 includes a post-training benchmark to measure your progress.
