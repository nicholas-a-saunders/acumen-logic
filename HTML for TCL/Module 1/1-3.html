How Aptitude Tests Are Scored

Percentiles, norm-referencing, and the strategic decisions that separate passing candidates from failing ones.

1

Raw Scores vs Norm-Referenced Scoring

Your aptitude test result is never reported as a simple mark out of total. Instead, your raw score is compared against a large reference group — typically thousands of graduates who've taken the same test — and converted into a percentile ranking.

Why This Matters

Two candidates answering 14/18 on different test versions could receive different percentiles — because the norm group and question difficulty differ. Your absolute score doesn't matter. Your relative ranking does.

2

What Percentiles Look Like

Most candidate scores cluster around the middle, with fewer at the extremes. This is the bell curve that determines your percentile.

Graduate Aptitude Score Distribution
10th 30th 50th 70th 90th
Typical pass zone (70th–80th+) Top performers (80th+)

Most UK finance firms require a percentile of 70th–80th or above. Some elite programmes (Goldman Sachs, McKinsey) effectively require 85th+ because of the candidate pool quality. There is no universal pass mark — each firm sets its own threshold, and some rank candidates rather than using a fixed cut-off.

3

Negative Marking

A common fear: "Will I lose marks for wrong answers?"

The answer for the vast majority of UK finance aptitude tests: no. SHL, Aon, Korn Ferry, and Cappfinity assessments do not use negative marking. A wrong answer scores the same as a blank — zero.

This has a critical strategic implication:

Golden Rule

Never leave a question blank. With 4 options, a random guess gives you a 25% chance of gaining a mark versus a 0% chance if you leave it blank. Even if you have no idea, always guess. Eliminate one wrong option and your odds rise to 33%.

Exception

A small number of tests (some Korn Ferry Aspects and certain specialist assessments) do apply a penalty for wrong answers. Your test invitation will state this clearly. If it's not mentioned, assume no negative marking — and guess freely.

4

Speed vs Accuracy — Which Wins?

This is the most important strategic question in aptitude testing. Let's look at two candidates taking the same 18-question SHL Numerical test.

Candidate A — Wins
72%
Attempted 15 of 18 questions
Got 13 correct
Accuracy: 87%
Candidate B — Loses
67%
Attempted 18 of 18 questions
Got 12 correct
Accuracy: 67%

Candidate A scores higher despite answering fewer questions. They focused on accuracy and skipped the questions they couldn't confidently answer. Candidate B rushed to complete everything and made more errors.

The lesson: accuracy is more valuable than completion. Tests are designed so that most candidates don't finish. Your goal isn't to answer every question — it's to maximise the number of correct answers.

The Exception

Aon (Cut-e) Scales tests are different. With ~15 seconds per question and no ability to go back, speed is paramount. For Aon tests specifically, answering quickly and moving on is the right strategy. We cover this in Lesson 6.2.

5

Verification Tests

Some firms (including several investment banks and the Big 4) invite candidates who pass the online test to re-sit a shorter supervised version at an assessment centre. This is normal — it's a check that your online score was genuine.

If your online performance was authentic, the verification test shouldn't concern you. The difficulty is usually comparable, and a moderate score drop is expected and accepted. A dramatic drop raises red flags.

6

Your Scoring Strategy

  1. Never leave a question blank — if there's no negative marking (and there usually isn't), a guess is always better than a blank.
  2. Prioritise accuracy over completion — getting 13/15 beats getting 12/18. Focus on questions you can answer correctly rather than rushing through everything.
  3. Use the 90-second rule — if you've spent more than 90 seconds on a single question without progress, flag it, make your best guess, and move on. Come back if time allows.
  4. Do the easy questions first — bank the marks you can get quickly. Return to harder questions with remaining time.

Key Takeaways

  • Your score is a percentile, not a percentage. You're ranked against a norm group of other graduate candidates.
  • Most UK finance tests require 70th–80th percentile or above to pass.
  • No negative marking in most tests (SHL, Aon, Cappfinity). Always guess rather than leave blank.
  • Accuracy beats speed. Getting 13/15 right outscores getting 12/18 right.
  • The 90-second rule: stuck for more than 90 seconds? Guess and move on.
