The UK Finance Test Landscape

Which employers use which tests, who makes them, and what you'll actually face on screen.

1

The Five Test Types

UK finance employers screen candidates using some combination of these five assessment categories. Not every firm uses all five — but knowing the full landscape means nothing catches you off guard.

1
Numerical Reasoning
Data interpretation from tables, charts, and graphs. Percentage calculations, ratios, currency conversions, profit & loss. Used by virtually all firms. This is the most common and most trainable test type.
2
Verbal Reasoning
True/False/Cannot Say format dominates. Read business passages and evaluate statements based only on the information given. Second most common after numerical.
3
Logical & Abstract Reasoning
Pattern recognition in shapes and sequences (inductive reasoning), rule-based deduction, number series. Used by many banks and consulting firms in varied formats.
4
Situational Judgement Tests (SJTs)
Workplace scenarios measuring communication, teamwork, decision-making, resilience, and commercial awareness. Increasingly common — over 50% of Big 4 assessments now include SJT elements.
5
Personality & Behavioural Assessments
Personality questionnaires and gamified assessments (e.g., Arctic Shores, Pymetrics). Measures cognitive traits, risk appetite, and cultural fit. Cannot be "passed" through study, but can be understood strategically.
2

The Major Test Providers

Employers don't write their own tests. They outsource to specialist providers. Each provider has a distinct format, interface, and timing structure — so knowing which provider your employer uses is just as important as knowing the content.

SHL SHL (Verify)
Used by: EY, Deloitte, KPMG, Barclays, Jefferies, Rothschild, Bank of America

The most widely used provider in UK finance. Offers both standard format (multiple-choice, 4–5 options) and Interactive format (drag-and-drop on charts and tables). You'll work with data tables, bar charts, pie charts, and line graphs. Questions appear one at a time and you can navigate back and forth.

Numerical: ~18 Qs / 25 mins Verbal: ~30 Qs / 19 mins Inductive: ~18 Qs / 25 mins
AON Aon (Cut-e Scales)
Used by: Morgan Stanley, Nomura, BNP Paribas, Deutsche Bank

Very different from SHL. The "Scales" format uses many short questions at a much faster pace. Questions are simpler individually but the speed pressure is intense. You typically cannot go back to previous questions — answer immediately and move on.

~50 Qs / ~12 mins ~15 secs per question No going back
KORN FERRY Korn Ferry (Talent-Q Aspects)
Used by: Citi, UBS, Macquarie

Adaptive difficulty. Questions get harder when you answer correctly and easier when you get them wrong. This means every candidate gets a different test. Significantly more answer options than SHL (sometimes 8–10 choices). You cannot go back to previous questions.

Numerical: ~12 Qs / 15 mins Verbal: ~15 Qs / 18 mins Adaptive difficulty
CAPPFINITY Cappfinity (Immersive Assessment)
Used by: HSBC, Deloitte

Immersive format. Cognitive reasoning and situational judgement combined in a single assessment. You'll face numerical, verbal, and inductive reasoning questions alongside workplace scenario decisions — all in one timed sitting of approximately 40–50 questions over ~50 minutes.

~40–50 Qs / ~50 mins Mixed cognitive + SJT
ARCTIC SHORES Arctic Shores (Game-Based)
Used by: PwC

No traditional questions at all. A series of mini-games measuring cognitive ability, risk appetite, learning speed, and behavioural preferences. Includes tasks like balloon inflation (risk tolerance), card sorting (rule learning), and pattern matching (processing speed). Covered in detail in Module 5.

~45–60 mins Gamified mini-games No MC questions
PYMETRICS Pymetrics (Neuroscience Games)
Used by: JP Morgan, Blackstone, Standard Chartered

Similar to Arctic Shores — neuroscience-based games measuring attention, effort, fairness, emotion, generosity, and decision-making. Used as a cultural and cognitive fit assessment rather than a knowledge test. Also covered in Module 5.

~25–30 mins 12 mini-games Behavioural profiling

Key Insight

Notice how Goldman Sachs and JP Morgan have moved away from traditional aptitude tests entirely — Goldman uses a HireVue video interview, and JP Morgan uses Pymetrics gamified assessment. If you're applying to these firms, Module 5 (Behavioural Assessment) is particularly important for you.

5

How to Research Your Employer

The tables above cover the most common employers, but if your target firm isn't listed — or if you want to double-check — here's how to find out exactly what you'll face.

  1. Check your application invitation. When you're invited to take the test, the email will typically name the provider (e.g., "You have been invited to complete an SHL assessment"). This is the most reliable source.
  2. Search for recent candidate experiences. Search "[Company name] aptitude test 2025" or "[Company name] online assessment 2026". The best sources are TheStudentRoom, WikiJob, Glassdoor, and Wall Street Oasis — candidates regularly post detailed descriptions of their test experience.
  3. Check the firm's careers page. Some firms (especially the Big 4) describe their application process step by step. Look for pages titled "Our Application Process" or "What to Expect".
Pro Tip

Once you know your provider, do their official practice test before anything else. SHL, Aon, Korn Ferry, and Cappfinity all offer free practice versions on their websites. This gives you the exact interface and timing you'll experience on test day. We cover provider-specific strategies in detail in Lesson 6.2.

What This Means for Your Preparation

Regardless of which provider your employer uses, the underlying skills are the same. Percentages, ratios, data interpretation, verbal reasoning, and logical thinking appear across all providers — just in different interfaces and at different speeds.

Here's how the course maps to your situation:

Modules 2–3 (Numerical Reasoning & Data Interpretation) prepare you for SHL, Aon, Korn Ferry, and Cappfinity cognitive tests. These skills are universal.

Module 4 (Verbal & Logical Reasoning) covers the verbal and logical formats used by all major providers.

Module 5 (SJT & Behavioural) is essential if your employer uses SJTs, personality tests, or gamified assessments (PwC, JP Morgan, Blackstone, Standard Chartered, HSBC, and the Big 4).

Module 6 includes provider-specific tips (Lesson 6.2) covering exactly how SHL Interactive differs from Aon Scales differs from Korn Ferry Aspects — the interface quirks, timing differences, and navigation rules that catch candidates off guard.

Key Takeaways

  • UK finance employers use five assessment categories: numerical, verbal, logical, SJT, and personality/behavioural.
  • Tests are built by specialist providers — SHL, Aon, Korn Ferry, Cappfinity, Arctic Shores, and Pymetrics are the main ones.
  • Knowing your provider matters. SHL gives you 83 seconds per question; Aon gives you 15. Same content, completely different strategy.
  • Some top employers (Goldman Sachs, JP Morgan) have moved away from traditional aptitude tests to video interviews and gamified assessments.
  • The core skills in Modules 2–4 are universal across all providers. Module 6 adds the provider-specific tactics.
