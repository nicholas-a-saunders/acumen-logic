Module 4 · Lesson 4.1

Deductive Reasoning & Syllogisms

If-then logic, valid vs invalid inferences, and the reasoning traps that catch most candidates in Barclays SHL Deductive and Korn Ferry Logical tests.

1

What Deductive Reasoning Tests Measure

Deductive reasoning is about drawing guaranteed conclusions from a set of given rules. Unlike numerical reasoning, there's no calculation — and unlike verbal reasoning, there's no passage to interpret. You're given rules and asked: what must be true?

This question type appears in SHL Deductive Reasoning tests (used by Barclays, Deloitte, Rothschild), Korn Ferry Logical Reasoning (Citi, Macquarie), and various bespoke assessments across investment banking and consulting.

Why This Matters

Deductive reasoning questions are designed to feel intuitive — and that's exactly what makes them dangerous. The answers that feel right are often logically invalid. This lesson teaches you to distinguish valid from invalid inferences before you encounter them under time pressure.

Three core skills are tested:

If-Then Logic → Syllogisms → Multi-Rule Deduction

Master these three patterns and you can handle any deductive question. Get them confused and you'll pick trap answers with total confidence — which is worse than guessing.

2

If-Then Logic: The Foundation

An if-then rule has two parts: a condition (the "if") and a consequence (the "then"). Understanding what you can and cannot conclude from such a rule is the single most important deductive skill.

The Rule
If P, then Q
P is the sufficient condition. Q is the necessary condition. P guarantees Q — but Q does not guarantee P.
Valid Inference — Modus Ponens

Rule: If an employee works more than 37.5 hours per week, they receive overtime pay.

Fact: John worked 42 hours this week.

Conclusion: John receives overtime pay. ✔

VALID — the condition is met, so the consequence must follow.

Valid Inference — Modus Tollens

Rule: If an employee works more than 37.5 hours per week, they receive overtime pay.

Fact: Sarah did not receive overtime pay.

Conclusion: Sarah did not work more than 37.5 hours. ✔

VALID — if the consequence didn't happen, the condition couldn't have been met.

The Two Traps

The following two inferences look reasonable — and are both invalid.

Invalid Inference — Affirming the Consequent

Rule: If an employee works more than 37.5 hours per week, they receive overtime pay.

Fact: Maria received overtime pay.

Conclusion: Maria worked more than 37.5 hours. ✘

INVALID — overtime pay could have other causes (contractual bonus, bank holiday premium, error correction). The rule doesn't say overtime is the only way to receive overtime pay.

Invalid Inference — Denying the Antecedent

Rule: If an employee works more than 37.5 hours per week, they receive overtime pay.

Fact: David worked 35 hours this week.

Conclusion: David did not receive overtime pay. ✘

INVALID — the rule says what happens when you DO work overtime. It says nothing about what happens when you don't. David might still receive overtime pay for other reasons.

Summary — If P, Then Q
P is true → Q must be true ✔   |   Q is false → P must be false ✔
Q is true → P might or might not be true ✘   |   P is false → Q might or might not be true ✘
3

Syllogisms: All, Some, and None

A syllogism is a logical argument with two premises and a conclusion. In aptitude tests, they typically use "All," "Some," or "No" statements about categories of people or things. The challenge is distinguishing conclusions that must be true from those that merely could be true.

Valid Syllogism

Premise 1: All analysts at Meridian Capital have passed CFA Level 1.

Premise 2: Tom is an analyst at Meridian Capital.

Conclusion: Tom has passed CFA Level 1. ✔

VALID — Tom is inside the "analysts" group, which is entirely inside the "CFA Level 1" group.

Why This Works — Venn Diagram
Tom is inside Analysts, which is entirely inside CFA Level 1 Holders. Therefore Tom must have CFA Level 1.
Invalid Syllogism — The Classic Trap

Premise 1: All analysts at Meridian Capital have passed CFA Level 1.

Premise 2: Rachel has passed CFA Level 1.

Conclusion: Rachel is an analyst at Meridian Capital. ✘

INVALID — Rachel could be an accountant, a portfolio manager, or work at a completely different firm. CFA Level 1 holders are not all analysts.

Why This Fails — Venn Diagram
Rachel is inside CFA Level 1 Holders — but she could be anywhere in that circle. She is not necessarily inside the Analysts subset.
The Core Principle

"All A are B" does not mean "All B are A." This is the single most common logical error in deductive reasoning tests. "All analysts have CFA" does not mean "Everyone with CFA is an analyst." The direction of the relationship matters.

Tests also use "Some" and "No" quantifiers:

Valid Conclusions

"No managers are interns"

→ No interns are managers ✔

"Some consultants are bilingual"

→ Some bilingual people are consultants ✔

Invalid Conclusions

"Some consultants are bilingual"

→ All consultants are bilingual ✘

"All partners are qualified"

→ All qualified people are partners ✘

4

Multi-Rule Deduction Problems

The hardest deductive questions give you 3–5 rules and a scenario, then ask which conclusion must follow. The technique is systematic elimination: apply each rule in sequence to narrow down the possibilities.

The Approach
Read all rules → Apply rules one by one → Eliminate what can't be true → What remains must be true
Never jump to a conclusion based on one rule alone. Chain the rules together.

Scenario: A consulting firm has the following policies for its London office:

Rule Policy
1 All senior consultants must hold a professional qualification (ACA, ACCA, or CFA).
2 If a consultant leads a client engagement, they must be a senior consultant.
3 No consultant without at least 3 years of experience may lead a client engagement.
4 James leads a client engagement for a FTSE 250 company.

Question: Which of the following must be true?

(A) James has more than 3 years of experience and holds a professional qualification.

(B) James is the most experienced consultant in the firm.

(C) James holds an ACA qualification specifically.

(D) All senior consultants lead client engagements.

Chain of Reasoning

From Rule 4: James leads a client engagement.

From Rule 2: Leading requires being a senior consultant → James is a senior consultant.

From Rule 1: Senior consultants must hold a professional qualification → James holds a professional qualification.

From Rule 3: Leading requires at least 3 years of experience → James has at least 3 years of experience.

Answer: (A) — James must have both 3+ years of experience and a professional qualification. ✔

Why the Other Options Fail

(B) is unsupported — "at least 3 years" does not mean "the most experienced." (C) is too specific — he holds a professional qualification, but it could be ACA, ACCA, or CFA. (D) reverses the rule — Rule 2 says leaders must be senior, not that all seniors must lead.

5

The Five Deductive Reasoning Traps

Every wrong answer in a deductive reasoning test corresponds to one of these five errors. Learn to recognise them and you'll eliminate trap answers instinctively.

  1. Reversing the direction — "All A are B" does not mean "All B are A." This is the most frequent trap. If all analysts have CFA, it does not follow that all CFA holders are analysts.
  2. Affirming the consequent — Knowing the outcome happened doesn't mean the specific cause occurred. "If it rains, the ground is wet. The ground is wet." You cannot conclude it rained — a sprinkler could be responsible.
  3. Denying the antecedent — Just because the condition isn't met doesn't mean the consequence doesn't occur. "If you work overtime, you get extra pay. You didn't work overtime." You might still get extra pay for another reason.
  4. Confusing "some" with "all" — "Some managers attended the conference" does not mean all did. And it does not mean most did. "Some" means at least one — nothing more.
  5. Going beyond the rules — Your own knowledge is irrelevant. If the rules don't say it, you cannot conclude it. A conclusion might be true in real life and still be unsupported by the given premises.
Test-Day Technique

When evaluating a conclusion, ask: "Can I construct a scenario where the premises are all true but the conclusion is false?" If yes, the conclusion is invalid. If you cannot construct such a scenario, the conclusion must be valid. This single test resolves nearly every deductive question.

6

Necessary vs Sufficient Conditions

This distinction underpins every if-then question. Confusing the two is the root cause of most deductive errors in aptitude tests.

Sufficient Condition

Definition: If this is true, the outcome is guaranteed.

Example: "Scoring above 85% is sufficient to pass." → If you score 90%, you definitely pass.

But you might also pass with 75% if the threshold is lower. The sufficient condition is not the only way.

Necessary Condition

Definition: This must be true for the outcome to occur — but alone, it may not be enough.

Example: "A degree is necessary to apply." → Without a degree, you cannot apply.

But having a degree alone doesn't guarantee your application succeeds. Other conditions may also apply.

The Quick Test
"If P, then Q" → P is sufficient for Q. Q is necessary for P.
Sufficient = guarantees the outcome. Necessary = required for the outcome but may not be enough alone.
In Real Tests

Questions often phrase these as: "Which of the following must be true?" (testing necessary conditions) or "Which of the following guarantees the outcome?" (testing sufficient conditions). Reading the question word precisely — must, could, guarantees, is possible — determines which logical tool to apply.

Key Takeaways

  • "If P, then Q" means P guarantees Q — but Q does not guarantee P. Never reverse the direction.
  • Affirming the consequent (Q is true, therefore P) and denying the antecedent (P is false, therefore not Q) are both invalid — and both common trap answers.
  • "All A are B" does not mean "All B are A." Use Venn diagrams to visualise the relationship when in doubt.
  • For multi-rule problems: read all rules first, chain them in sequence, eliminate options one by one.
  • The counterexample test — "Can I imagine a scenario where premises are true but conclusion is false?" — resolves nearly every deductive question.
  • Distinguish necessary from sufficient: "must" tests necessity, "guarantees" tests sufficiency. Read question wording precisely.
